Abstract

A major challenge in understanding the process of nucleus-nucleus interactions is the examination of the processes that occur in the participant and spectator areas of interacting nuclei, considering the central nature of the reactions. Nearly thresholdless detection of the secondary charged particles is made possible by nuclear emulsion detectors (NED), which provide a complete 4 angular coverage. This work is mainly concerned with the multiplicity distributions (MD) and fluctuation of the average multiplicities of secondary charged particles (slow proton, fast proton, and shower) brought about by the collision of -nuclei with emulsion nuclei at 1 A GeV. The MD of these particles has been computed by use of a modified cascade evaporation model (MCEM). The MD of each of the several charged secondary particles is correlated and analyzed. The observation demonstrates that the theoretical calculation results for the average multiplicities of shower particles, fast and slow protons agree well with the experimental data. Correlations seen experimentally between the multiplicities of different emitted particles are faithfully reproduced by the MCEM. There is good agreement between the experimental data and the theoretical calculation results.

Keywords: RHIC, NED, MCEM, MD, Multiplicity correlations, Slow protons, Fast protons, Shower particles

Subject terms: Nuclear physics, Particle physics

Introduction

The study of relativistic nucleus–nucleus (A–A) and hadrons–nucleus (h–A) collisions has drawn a lot of attention from researchers in the past decade, following the prediction of a new phase of matter known as Quark–Gluon Plasma (QGP)1–5. In order to investigate a cardinal equation in nuclear and sub-nuclear physics, heavy-ion collisions yield information on the deep features of nuclear matter, such as quarks, gluons, and nuclear matter density, among other things6–9. In recent years, a number of studies have examined the properties of secondary charged particles generated in high-energy A–A collisions10–16. The MD of secondary particles generated in interactions with NED nuclei has been studied in this study since it is important for finding information on the physical processes of their origins17–19. The pattern is useful for verifying that a theoretical model can accurately describe nuclear processes. A–A interactions occur in three phases at high energy, starting with the pionization step, which produces shower particles. A large number of the particles released during the second stage of recoil nucleons are grey particles. The evaporation of the excited nuclei is the stage that culminates in the emission of black particles in the third stage.

For the investigation of heavy-ion collisions, there exist several models20–24. Such models include the cascade evaporation model (CEM)20,21,24. In order to explain the ubiquitous occurrence of relativistic A–A collisions, this model is thought to be the most often used. According to CEM, multiparticle generation is thought to be caused by incoherent meson-nucleon and A–A interactions, whereas the final hadrons are formed instantaneously. One or two compound systems are created after the collision and eventually decay into the ultimate multi-particle states, which have longer lives than the initial phases. It takes a certain amount of time, known as the formation time, for hadrons to be produced. Consequently, the additional parameter of formation time was incorporated into the multi-particle production process by developing the MCEM for A–A collision24. Two different approaches can be used to complete this process. The first one, which was supplied by Ranft25, is predicated on the idea that the rapidly generated particles are incapable of undergoing any kind of nuclear secondary interaction. Experimental details

In Darmstadt, Germany, GSI supplied the NED. The nuclei of the projectiles have an incidence energy of 1 A GeV and are composed of 98 to 95 and 2 to 5 impurities26. It was determined that the NED plates were cm3. The two most popular scanning techniques, line and volume scanning, were employed to scan the events using an Olympus BH-2 binocular microscope26–29. The primary parts of this microscope are a variety of dry and oil immersion objectives with magnifications of 10X, 20X, 40X, 60X, and 100X. Using a 100X objective, sandalwood oil was sprayed to remove air between the lens and the NED plate. The line scanning technique follows tracks along their length until they come into contact with one of the photographic emulsion materials, break through any two of the emulsion’s surfaces, or come to a stop in the plate26–29. It is important to consider background events since they could interact anywhere on the plate. However, when we return to look for their imprints on the NED plate, we find no trace of them and they have vanished. Using volume scanning, low energy events have been investigated. After detecting the events using volume scanning, we monitor the event tracks to verify their accuracy27,28. Using both scanning methods we have scanned total 750 events. In this study we have used 700 events out of 750 events for physics analysis.

A number of criteria were used to identify the data following the event collection, such as range (L), relative velocity (), and normalized grain density (g*)23. The shower particles from the participation region of the two interacting nuclei are identified as Ns, with g* < 1.4 and > 0.7. Outside the projectile fragmentation angle (), these are relativistic particles that are singly charged and have a K.E. more than 400 MeV. The target spectator zone is exhibiting grey particles, as indicated by Ng, with 0.7 < > 0.3, 6.0 > g* > 1.4 and L > 3 mm. Nb is the quantity of black particles that exit the spectator zone of interest. Their g* > 6.0, < 0.3, and L < 3 mm are all present. The collective term for black and grey particles is strongly ionized charged particles (Nh)29.

A crucial source of information on particle production methods is the multiplicity correlation (MC) between the many secondary charged particles generated in the high-energy interaction of A–A. The MC is one of the best ways to confirm that a model is adequate for explaining the nuclear interaction by obtaining the correlations among the secondary particles released in the A–A interactions. The Nb, Ng, and Ns correlations are shown as functions of Nh in Figs 5a–c. As can be observed in Fig. 5, all Fig. 5a–c exhibit high dependence on Nh that grow linearly with Nh, with the exception of Nb. In this instance, the relation becomes almost constant (plateau) within the experimental errors for Nh > 21 and grows linearly with Nh up to 21.

The MC of Ns with Ng and Nb are shown in Fig 6a and b. The number of interacting projectile nucleons causes Ns to rise linearly, and the free energy in the C.M.S. of the colliding nuclei determines Ns. See Fig. 6a. Ns also grows when the number of interacting projectile nucleons increases since it might be dependent on that number. This linear dependence also prompted us to take into account Ng as a measure of the number of interacting nucleons and the inter-nuclear collisions of the projectile nucleons. Therefore, the significant correlation between the average number of shower particles generated and the number of grey particles emitted suggests that the number of grey particles can be used as a proxy for the frequency of intranuclear collisions26. The dependence of Ns on Nb is linear up to a specific value of Nb close to 17, after which the relationship reaches a plateau, as seen in Fig. 6b. This also supports the findings of other reported works17.

Conclusion

In order to compare the experimental results with the related theoretical calculations of MCEM, the -emulsion interactions at 1 A GeV were examined. When compared to the relevant experimental values, the average multiplicities of the various released particles computed using the MCEM shows good agreement. Additionally, the MCEM accurately replicates the correlations between the multiplicities of the various released particles that were seen experimentally.

The dependence on Nh in Ns and Ng are significant and rise linearly with Nh, with the exception of Nb, as Fig. 5 illustrates. In the case of Nb, it rises linearly with Nh until 21. For Nh > 21, the relation becomes roughly constant (plateau) within the experimental errors. Additionally, as seen in Fig. 6b, the relationship between Ns and Nb is linear up to a point at which Nb approaches 17, at which point it plateaus. At specific values of Ns, the critical temperature rises with projectile energy and stays constant with increasing projectile mass number, but the temperature of the nucleus is independent of the number of impacts. This is because the system may be pumped with too much energy with larger values of Ns. This energy could therefore be used up in the target nucleus system’s transition phase.
