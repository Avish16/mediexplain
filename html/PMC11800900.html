Abstract

Purpose

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most common cancer worldwide, and its incidence and mortality rates are increasing every year. The intestinal microbiota has been called the "neglected organ" and there is growing evidence that the intestinal microbiota and its metabolites can be used in combination with immunotherapy, radiotherapy and chemotherapy to greatly enhance the treatment of colorectal cancer and to address some of the side effects and adverse effects of these therapies. Antibiotics have great potential to eliminate harmful microbiota, control infection, and reduce colorectal cancer side effects. However, the use of antibiotics has been a highly controversial issue, and numerous retrospective studies have shown that the use of antibiotics affects the effectiveness of treatment (especially immunotherapy). Understanding the bi-directional role of the gut microbiota and antibiotics will further enhance our research into the diagnosis and treatment of cancer.

Methods

We searched the "PubMed" database and selected the following keywords "intestinal microbiota, antibiotics, treatment, prevention, colorectal cancer". In this review, we discuss the role of the intestinal microbiota in immunotherapy, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, diagnosis, and prevention of CRC. We also conclude that the intestinal microbiota and antibiotics work together to promote the treatment of CRC through a bidirectional effect.

Results

We found that the intestinal microbiota plays a key role in promoting immunotherapy, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, diagnosis and prevention of CRC. In addition, gut microbiota and antibiotic interactions could be a new strategy for CRC treatment.

Conclusion

The bi-directional role of the intestinal microbiota and antibiotics plays a key role in the prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of colorectal cancer.

Keywords: Intestinal microbiota, Antibiotics, Treatment, Prevention, Colorectal cancer

Introduction

CRC is the world's third most common cancer. Despite major advances in CRC therapy, CRC remains the world's second-largest cause of death from cancer (Bray et al. 2018). CRC incidence is expected to dramatically over the next 20 years as a result of changing dietary choices and premature antibiotic exposure (Arnold et al. 2017). Therefore, the future path must focus on is CRC prevention, detection, and treatment. The gut microbiota is regarded the "neglected organ," and current research estimates that it includes nearly all body's microbiota flora (Tilg et al. 2018). Simultaneously, there is mounting evidence that gut microbiota plays an important role in CRC development, prevention, and treatment. CRC treatment remains dependent on surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy. With the advancement of immunotherapy in recent years, CRC treatment has advanced to a new level. The intestinal microbiota is strongly linked to CRC treatment and has proven to be quite valuable, particularly in immunotherapy. Tumor immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) can re-awaken immune cells' anti-tumor activity and play a key role in tumor immunotherapy (Tsai and Hsu 2017; Ganesh et al. 2019). Recent research has discovered that ICIs and the intestinal microbiota interacts via linked biological pathways, which can largely overcome tumor immunotherapy tolerance and make significant progress in the treatment of CRC (Ooki et al. 2021; Makaremi et al. 2021). However, the intestinal microbiota does not play an entirely active role in CRC treatment, where immune checkpoint inhibitor toxicity has been a chronic issue (Myers 2018). Although antibiotic use is beneficial to some extent, it can disrupt gut flora and impair the effectiveness of immune checkpoint inhibitors. Recent studies, however, have discovered that rational antibiotic selection, control of antibiotic duration, and use of special antidotes can well avoid the disruption of intestinal microbiota, and to some extent, can complement the use of ICIs, reducing their toxicity, better controlling tumor symptoms, and improving patient quality of survival. Therefore, this work aims to describe the function of intestinal microbiota in colorectal cancer immunotherapy, radiation, and chemotherapy, with an emphasis on the involvement of intestinal microbiota and ICIs. This research also outlines how the intestinal microbiota might interact with antibiotics via antibiotic treatments to improve CRC treatment. Finally, we discuss the function of intestinal microbiota in CRC prevention and diagnosis to develop a more effective CRC prevention, diagnosis, and treatment system.

The role of intestinal microbiota in tumor therapy

The role of intestinal microbiota in immunotherapy

Immunotherapy for colorectal cancer mostly consists of immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy and adoptive T cell (ACT) therapy with T cells, with ICI therapy being the most common in recent years. ICI therapy has shown good effectiveness in trials with mismatch repair-deficient and microsatellite instability-high (dMMR-MSI-H) CRC tumors but not in other CRC types (Sahin et al. 2019). There are currently three ACT types: tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TLS), chimeric antigen receptors (CAR), and modified T cell receptors (TCR). Among them, chimeric antigen receptor T (CAR T) cell treatment is the most modern and well-studied technique (Johdi and Sukor 2020). Here, we focus on summarizing the mechanisms of the function of intestinal microbiota in immune checkpoint inhibitor treatments as well as CAR T cell therapies in this section. This will also provide us with fresh ideas for CRC immunotherapy.

Immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy

ICI improves immune cell anti-tumor activity by blocking certain regulatory receptors, such as T cell programmed cell death 1 (PDCD1)/ CD274 molecule (CD274) and cytotoxic T lymphocyte-related protein 4 (CTLA4) (Sharma and Allison 2015; Pardoll 2012). The programmed death 1 receptor and its ligand (PDCD1/ CD274), which in its normal condition inhibits the over-activation of the immune response, therefore safeguarding the body's normal function (Shindo et al. 2015). The combination of CD274 (on the surface of tumor cells) and PDCD1 (on the surface of immune cells) inhibits immune cell activity via two major routes. When PDCD1 and CD274 bind, they phosphorylate immunoreceptor tyrosine-based switch motif (ITSM) and promote the expression of the phosphatase SHP-1/SHP-2. (Li et al. 2015). This process inactivates or phosphorylates ZAP70 on the TCR, and PI3K pathway controlled by CD28 is blocked, resulting in immune cell function inhibition (Xie et al. 2019; Barclay et al. 2018; Zhang et al. 2021). Another strategy for suppressing T cell activity is to disrupt PI3K/AKT/mTOR and Ras/MEK/ERK signaling pathways, which impede CK2-mediated phosphatase and PTEN phosphorylation (Kalathur et al. 2015; Borgo and Ruzzene 2019; Shimizu et al. 2012). CTLA4, on the other hand, achieves tumor immune evasion by reducing Treg activity via B7 ligand binding and suppressing TCR immunological synapses to prevent mutual recognition with antigen-presenting cells (APCs) (Masteller et al. 2000). Furthermore, CTLA4 can reduce IL-2 production by blocking AKT pathway via PP2A (Spranger et al. 2014). In response to these immune checkpoint inhibitory effects, ICI development and application have greatly aided the process of tumor immunotherapy, primarily by stimulating the release of IL-12 from dendritic cells to activate clt or by preventing the activation of T regulatory cells (Tregs), which ultimately acts against tumors. A considerable number of research in recent years have discovered that gut flora plays an important role in cancer immunotherapy. Sivan et al. chose melanoma mice from two separate labs (Jackson Laboratory (JAX) and Taconic Farms (TAC)) with the same genetic background but distinct gut flora. They discovered that JAX mice were more resistant to cancers than TAC mice, and that this difference in antitumor ability vanished when JAX mice's fecal microbiota were transferred into TAC mice. Furthermore, they discovered that combining JAX mouse fecal samples with ICIs led to more active T cell expression in mice and a considerably stronger antitumor impact than ICIs alone (Sivan et al. 2015). As a result of this historic breakthrough, research on the use of intestinal microbiota to enhance the efficacy of ICIs was initiated. Here, we summarize the mechanisms of how the intestinal microbiota enhances the efficacy of ICIs in immunotherapy (Fig. 1). The current study discovered that combining bifidobacteria with anti-CD274 immunotherapy increases the efficiency of ICI by increasing dendritic cell expression and activating the effector function of CD8+ T cells (Sivan et al. 2015). Lactobacillus acidophilus improves anti-CTLA4 immunotherapy in a mouse model of CRC by increasing CD8+ T cells and decreasing Tregs and M2 macrophages (Zhuo et al. 2019). Mager et al. discovered that in a mouse model of CRC, B. pseudolongum strain generated inosine, which stimulated cDC-dependent Th1 cell development, considerably increasing the efficacy of ICI (Mager and Burkhard 2020). Furthermore, it was revealed in Bifidobacterium pseudolongum that the produced inosine stimulated the cAMP-PKA-pCREB pathway via T cell-specific adenosine 2A receptor (A2AR) signaling, whereas the synergistic effect of TCR resulted in high IL-12 expression, stimulated Th1 cell differentiation, and enhanced the efficacy of ICI (Lasek et al. 2015; He et al. 2017; Cekic and Linden 2014; Lioux et al. 2016). Using polymycin B (pmB) to kill Gram-negative microbiota in the intestinal flora to lower the production of LPS, a metabolite of flora, was found to relieve tumor suppressive microenvironment of CRC while increasing T cell infiltration to enable anti-CD274 therapy (Song et al. 2018). When using ICIs, we inevitably have colitis, and this colitis under the toxic reaction of ICIs will seriously affect the therapeutic effect (Michot et al. 2016). Song et al. discovered that Bifidobacterium bifidum changed the makeup of gut flora in a Treg-dependent way. The altered intestinal microbiota increased the suppressive activity of intestinal Tregs via IL-10 and might greatly reduce colitis caused by ICIs (Sun and Luo 2020). Intestinal microbiota and metabolites improve ICI efficacy by increasing the expression and differentiation of DCs and Th1 cells while decreasing the number of Tregs and M2 macrophages, eventually encouraging CD8+ T cell proliferation. Meanwhile, the gut microbiota reduces ICI toxicity by limiting the evolution of inflammation, hence reducing the harmful effects of ICI therapy and improving patients' quality of life. The intestinal microbiota can act as a solid pillar for the usage of matching and ICIs in the direction of ICIs therapy for colorectal cancer.

CAR T therapy and intestinal microbiota

CAR T therapy is an innovative therapeutic approach that is high efficiency in colorectal cancer immunotherapy. CAR T cell treatment involves collecting T cells from patients that have been genetically engineered to detect antigens on the tumor surface in an MHC-restricted way and react to numerous tumor cells (Fesnak et al. 2016; Gross et al. 1989; Guedan et al. 2019). Current CAR-T therapy may have potential targets. (1) Guanylyl cyclase 2 C (GUCY2C): GUCY2C CAR-T cells can effectively decrease tumor growth in a mouse model (Magee et al. 2018). the epithelial cell adhesion molecule (EpCAM): In a mouse model, EpCAM-dependent CAR T cells can release tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α) and interferon gamma (IFN-γ), which limit tumor proliferation and development (Li et al. 2021). (2) Carcinoembryonic (CEA): the best available marker for diagnosis and prognosis is CEA, a glycoprotein that is widely produced in colorectal cancer (Han et al. 2020; Zhang et al. 2017). (3) Human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) and tumor-associated glycoprotein 72 (TAG-72): HER2 and TAG-72-specific CAR T cells can significantly improve immune function and anti-tumor effectiveness as tumor-associated targets (Siena et al. 2018; Cho et al. 2019). (4) Natural killer group 2 member D (NKG2D): this receptor promotes the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IFN-, IL-2) and is a strong predictor of poor prognosis (Deng et al. 2019). (5) Mesothelin (MSLN), Mucin-1 (MUC-1), and Cluster of Differentiation 133 (CD133): these receptors have all been linked to an increased risk of colorectal cancer recurrence and susceptibility to distant metastasis (Klampatsa and Dimou 2021; Nabavinia et al. 2017; Akbari et al. 2020; Aparicio et al. 2021). The gut microbiota and its metabolites may contribute to its effectiveness via appropriate targets on CAR T cells. Metabolites of gut microbiota (e.g., LPS) also contribute to ACT effectiveness via toll-like receptor (TLR) 4 signaling (Paulos et al. 2007). How the intestinal microbiota enhances T cell function to promote the efficacy of CAR T therapy? The following mechanisms might exist. Enterococci, via lipophosphopiridic lysophosphatidic  acid, boost TLR2 receptor expression on T cells and increase IL-2, TNF, and IFN production by CD8+ T cells, hence promoting CAR T effectiveness (Uribe-Herranz et al. 2018; Salerno and Freen-van Heeren 2019). T cell activity is influenced by tryptophan metabolites produced by gut microbiota via the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) (Rothhammer and Quintana 2019). Bile acid metabolites of intestinal microbiota can enhance the activity of T cells via the vitamin D receptor (VDR), Farnesoid X receptor (FXR) (Schubert et al. 2021; Campbell and McKenney 2020; Hang et al. 2019; Campbell et al. 2020). Furthermore, short-chain fatty acids derived from intestinal microbial metabolites promote T cell development and function via G protein-coupled receptors 41, 43, and 109 (GPRs) and histone deacetylases (HDACs) (Schubert et al. 2021). The value of intestinal microbiota in radiotherapy and chemotherapy

Chemotherapy remains the first-line therapeutic choice for colorectal cancer. Given the relevance of intestinal microbiota in CRC development and treatment, many studies have been conducted to determine how intestinal microbiota control gastrointestinal toxicity, inflammation, and drug resistance caused by chemotherapy. It is well known and understood that gut microorganisms play two roles. On the one hand, it inhibits the efficacy of chemotherapy. Gamma proteus was able to convert chemotherapeutic medicines into inactive forms in a mouse model of CRC, rendering tumors ineffective to Gefitinib therapy (Geller and Barzily-Rokni 2017). In contrast, when combined with the antibiotic ciprofloxacin, the chemotherapeutic agent’s efficacy was restored. This is indirect evidence that intestinal microbiota can reduce the side effects and improve the efficacy of chemotherapeutic drugs. This hypothesis is now supported by a vast number of research. Probiotics based on tryptic and oligofibrates have been demonstrated to improve the efficacy of chemotherapy medicines, such as 5-fluorouracil (5-FU), doxorubicin, vincristine, cyclophosphamide, methotrexate, and others (Taper and Roberfroid 2005). 5-FU is a commonly used chemotherapeutic drug in CRC treatment. 5-FU, in combination with probiotics, such as Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus subspecies Bi-04TM, and Lactobacillus paracasei, has been shown to considerably boost the anti-tumor effectiveness of chemotherapy medicines (Genaro et al. 2019). The combination of chemotherapeutic drugs, such as 5-FU and Lactobacillus paracasei, effectively prevented the development of CRC in a mouse model of CRC (Chang and Pan 2018). Furthermore, animals transplanted with Lactobacillus casei reduced chemotherapeutic-induced intestinal damage and inflammation by blocking NF-B pathway and IL-6 production (Paliany et al. 2018). Fusobacterium nucleatum increased the effects of treatment with 5-FU or OXA in CRC patients by activating immunological pathways such as TLR4/MyD88 (Yu et al. 2017). Despite the significant limitations of chemotherapeutic medicines, the sensible introduction of intestinal microbiota is a well-thought-out path that can be exploited in future to enhance chemotherapy effects.

Radiation treatment has a strong correlation with immunological response. Radiation-irradiated tumor cells boost the antitumor impact by reducing the expression of CD80 and CD86, increasing the number of effector T cells, and inhibiting Treg activity via APC activation (Herrera et al. 2017). Furthermore, radiation enhances T cell recruitment of chemotherapeutic factors and transforms tumor microenvironment (TME) to an inflammatory state, resulting in a greater proclivity of T cells to attack tumor tissue (Barker et al. 2015). While radiation treatment has anti-cancer benefits, it also promotes cancer and causes gastrointestinal inflammation, bleeding, and other adverse effects (Ma et al. 2019). Surprisingly, gut microbiota can be employed to increase radiation efficiency while also reducing negative effects. By sequencing the 16S rRNA gene in a mouse model, significant changes in intestine microbial composition in the radiation background were observed, indicating that intestinal microbiota may play a larger role in radiation treatment (Zhang et al. 2019). The abundance of Clostridium and Roseburia in the microbiome of patients with enteropathy rose considerably after radiation. Rectal Roseburia and Propionibacterium also showed this alteration (Reis Ferreira et al. 2019). Furthermore, probiotics like Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium have been demonstrated to prevent gastrointestinal toxic responses such colitis and diarrhea (Ciorba et al. 2012). The delivery of particular microbiota to radiation-treated mice utilizing a fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) technique greatly restored the integrity and function of the gastrointestinal mucosa and considerably boosted the number of immune cells in the treated animals (Cui et al. 2017). Using probiotics during radiotherapy treatment has been proven in clinical investigations in cancer patients to minimize radiation-induced intestinal damage. Surprisingly, removing fungal colonies from the intestine significantly enhanced the anticancer response induced by radiation. Antifungal drugs used in conjunction with radiation resulted in a reduction in CD206+F4/80+suppressor macrophages and a substantial rise in CD8+ T cells expressing Granzyme B (Shiao et al. 2021). The intestinal microbiota has an important role in the radiotherapy of CRC, significantly enhancing the efficacy of chemotherapy and improving the quality of life of patients.

Synergistic effects between antibiotics and intestinal microbiota

In recent years, the significance of the intestinal microbiota in CRC has gradually caught wider attention, and antibiotic treatment has been extensively researched as a means of directly killing and inhibiting the intestinal microbiota. Long-term and uncontrolled antibiotic use, on the other hand, has resulted in the formation of a high number of antibiotic resistance and pathogenic microbiota colonization, severe disturbance of the microbial ecosystem, and a greatly increased risk of CRC development. There are various theories and mechanism by which pathogenic microbiota might contribute to the development of CRC, when drugs impair microbial homeostasis, according to a vast number of studies: (1) The "driver-passenger" theory: Tjalsma et al. suggested the "driver-passenger" paradigm, in which intestinal microbiota are referred to as "microbiota drivers" (driver microbiota), which can colonize tumor tissues due to certain genotypes and virulence profiles and can be transferred through damaged tumor tissues (Tjalsma et al. 2012). Following that, the microbiota driver is replaced by a passenger bacterium with a competitive growth advantage, resulting in CRC development (Avril and DePaolo 2021). The "driver-passenger" model was intensively investigated in the years that followed, based on a thorough understanding of the prospective "drivers and candidate passenger microorganisms." Dejea et al. found that by depending on the "drivers," Eubacterium bacterium (ETBF) and thick-walled species (pks+ E. coli) Promote high secretion of IL-17, causing colonic epithelial DNA damage and inducing CRC formation (Dejea et al. 2018). Furthermore, these microbiota reduce E-calcitonin levels in the colon, increase intestinal mucosal permeability, produce high quantities of IL-6, promote signal transduction activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) expression, and induce an inflammatory response, thus favoring CRC development (Li et al. 2019). (2) Inflammation. Gut microorganisms can promote cancer progression by creating an inflammatory microenvironment. Long et al. discovered that anaerobic bifidomicrobiota causes CRC by stimulating pro-inflammatory cytokine production, attracting tumor-infiltrating immune cells (e.g., MDSC and TAM), and boosting immunosuppressive microenvironment development (Long et al. 2019). F. nucleatum can attract tumor-infiltrating immune cells, activate the NF-κB pathway, and promote IL-17 secretion to create an inflammatory microenvironment that promotes the development of CRC (Ye et al. 2017). (3) Toxicity factors: the presence of flagella, trichomes, and adhesins promotes microbiota adherence, and pathogenic microbiota have pathogenic virulence factors that accelerate CRC growth. F. nucleatum promotes CRC by secreting clostridial adhesin A (FadA) and choosing E-calmodulin for attachment, triggering the β-linked protein signaling cascade and breaking the outer intestinal mucosal barrier (Nithianantham et al. 2009). Furthermore, adhesin Fap2 on the surface of F. nucleatum can decrease T cell function and increase NK cell toxicity by modulating the pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-8 and CXCL1, so producing an immunosuppressive microenvironment (Coppenhagen-Glazer et al. 2015). E. coli's afa and eae adhesins, as well as Salmonella's AvrA protein, disrupt the integrity of the outer intestinal mucosal epithelium and cause CRC (Lu et al. 2010). (4) Oxidative stress: the formation of pro-oxidant molecules [e.g., reactive oxygen species (ROS) and reactive nitrogen species (RNS)] that limits efficient antioxidant defenses causes oxidative stress. In the presence of chronic inflammation caused by dysbiosis of the intestinal microbiota, oxidative stress is exacerbated because ROS and RNS are released in large quantities by inflammatory cells and can lead to irreversible cellular damage, such as single- and double-stranded DNA breaks, protein aggregation or fragmentation (Ray and Kidane 2016). The gut microbiota can also directly generate ROS and RNS. Macrophages can encourage Enterococcus faecalis to create superoxide, whereas E. faecalis can make hydroxyl radicals, both of which can eventually damage DNA in epithelial cells and cause CRC (Meira et al. 2008).

Furthermore, antibiotic treatment appears to cause certain inevitable adverse effects in several trials. The systemic response, for example, reduces the amounts of immune cells while also affecting their function and activity. The amount of Th1, Th2, Th17, and T cells is substantially reduced, increasing the likelihood of unfavorable responses in the organism (Becattini et al. 2016). This immunological dysfunction appears to be linked to decreased antimicrobial peptide expression, dysregulated Toll-like receptor (TLR) signaling, decreased IFN-γ expression, and low mucosal IgA production as a result of intestinal microbiota disruption (Robak et al. 2018). These processes lead to impaired intestinal mucosal barrier function and persistent inflammation, both of which contribute significantly to the development of cancer and its associated side effects. Furthermore, multiple retrospective investigations have indicated that antibiotic therapy reduces the effectiveness of ICI. Vétizou et al. revealed that an antibiotic cocktail of ampicillin, mucin, and streptomycin decreased the efficiency of anti-CTLA4 and accelerated tumor formation in a CRC mice model (Vétizou et al. 2015). Similarly, anti-CTLA4 treatment in a vancomycin-treated mice model of colitis resulted in more severe adverse effects (Wang et al. 2018). Tinsley et al. revealed that cumulative antibiotic exposure prior to ICI therapy reduced ICI effectiveness. Patients using cumulative antibiotics had significantly lower progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) (Tinsley et al. 2020). Huang et al. discovered a significant bond between antibiotic usage and poor PFS and OS in a meta-analysis (Huang et al. 2019). Antibiotics reduce the efficacy of immunosuppression by encouraging the growth of certain pathogenic microbiota (e.g., Clostridium perfringens nucleus, Enterobacter rotundus, and Proteomicrobiota spp.), which reduce the efficacy of immunotherapy by reducing CD8+ T cell recruitment and inhibiting TLR4 expression on IFN-γ and NK cells (Kostic et al. 2013). At present, findings indicating antibiotic use reduce ICI efficacy come mostly from numerous retrospective investigations, and the exact mechanisms are unknown. The effect of antibiotic use on the effectiveness of ICIs and CRC should be studied further in future.

Despite the fact that a vast number of retrospective studies appear to be elaborating on the adverse function of antibiotics in CRC treatment. Surprisingly, as antibiotic research has intensified in recent years, sensible antibiotic administration may mainly avoid the harmful consequences in CRC therapy, and antibiotics do not have an entirely negative role. First, antibiotic use alters the intestinal microbiota, which can slow CRC progression (Zackular et al. 2013). This beneficial impact is mostly due to the removal of Bacteroides fragilis and microbiota associated with mucin breakdown, as well as the decrease of chronic inflammation (DeStefano Shields et al. 2016; Ijssennagger et al. 2015). Antibiotic therapy has also been linked to a reduction in CRC proliferation and invasion. Metronidazole treatment eradicated Clostridium perfringens and inhibited CRC growth (Bullman et al. 2017). The same study revealed that antibiotic therapy lowered the microbiota burden in the tumor and assisted tumor growth suppression in a mouse model of CRC (Triner et al. 2019). Second, the type and time of antibiotic administration are critical in using antibiotics in cancer treatment. The current study focuses on the influence of antibiotic therapy on the efficacy of ICI via a mechanism that is not entirely evident, but is most likely: antibiotics affect the efficacy of ICI by modifying the makeup of the intestinal microbiota. This also gives a concept for antibiotic therapy, and the investigation of antibiotic antidotes, as well as managing the kind and duration of antibiotic administration, will solve this problem to the maximum extent possible. Ahmed et al. discovered that, whereas broad-spectrum antibiotics can impair the efficiency of ICI, narrow-spectrum antibiotics do not affect the efficacy of ICI and can effectively prevent the incidence of side effects (Ahmed et al. 2018). In terms of the timing of antibiotic administration, early administration has less effect on ICI (Ueda et al. 2019). Intestinal microbiota, such as Fusobacterium nucleatum, Enterococcus faecalis, and Escherichia coli, have been linked to CRC development (Bashir et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2008; Veziant et al. 2016). Eliminating these microorganisms with narrow-spectrum antibiotics may dramatically lower the likelihood of CRC development. Pharmacological antidotes, such as benzbromarone and bicoumarins, may minimize the collateral damage associated with macrolide and tetracycline antibiotic therapy, easing CRC treatment (Maier and Goemans 2021). Furthermore, antibiotic use has resulted in the development of multidrug-resistant microbiota, increasing the risk of infection, which has been a challenging problem to address (Kaushik et al. 2019). Probiotics can be carried by FMT to impact the colonization of resistant microbiota, ultimately reducing antibiotic resistance. Antibiotic use in hospitalized patients enhances the generation of drug-resistant microbiota, but probiotic strains (e.g., Lactobacillus plantarum) can limit the colonization of naturally resistant pathogens (e.g., Acinetobacter baumannii, Pseudomonas aeruginosa) (Singhi and Kumar 2016; Soltan Dallal et al. 2017). The interaction between gut microbiota and antibiotics will be critical in CRC combination treatment.

Antibiotic use is a double-edged sword, causing an imbalance in the balance of gut microbiota on the one hand while also being able to prevent the occurrence of side effects such as infections and enhance patients' quality of life on the other. This also points to a new therapeutic direction for using antibiotics and gut microbiota in the treatment of CRC in future. First, consideration is given to the problem of drug-resistant and harmful microorganisms as a result of antibiotic usage. Modifying enzyme inhibitors, membrane permeabilizers, and efflux pump inhibitors (EPIs) are now being used as remedies. Modifying enzyme inhibitors are classified into two types (Laws et al. 2019). β-lactamase inhibitors (BLI) and aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes work to disrupt microbiota detoxification enzymes and interfere with drug-resistant microbiota's cell wall formation. Membrane permeabilizers make the inner membranes of microbiota organelles more permeable, allowing antibiotics to enter the cell more easily (Zabawa et al. 2016). EPIs prevent microbiota antibiotic excretion from the cell, raising intracellular antibiotic concentrations and boosting antibiotic effectiveness (Poole 2007). Pharmacological antidotes (e.g., benzbromarone and dicoumarins) are also a novel avenue that can be employed to prevent collateral damage from macrolide and tetracycline antibiotic therapy and facilitate CRC treatment (Maier and Goemans 2021). Furthermore, using coupled FMT techniques and probiotics (e.g., Bacteroides fragilis, Bifidobacterium shortum, Akkermansia muciniphila, and Pausnitzii faecalis) promotes CCR9+CXCR3+CD4+T lymphocytes into the tumor microenvironment to improve anti-tumor capacity, inhibit CRC progression, and improve patient survival and quality of life (Huang et al. 2019; Frankel et al. 2017). The timing, type, and mode of antibiotic delivery should also be addressed. Using narrow-spectrum selective oral antibiotics one month before treatment or as soon as feasible before therapy can reduce disturbance of the gut microbiota balance and avoid unpleasant effects and infections associated with surgery and other therapies. Several clinical trials (NCT01740947, NCT03856671, and NCT03563586) have been conducted to investigate the influence of prophylactic antibiotic usage prior to CRC surgery on CRC treatment. The results are relatively optimistic. The findings are generally encouraging. Based on the above experimental results and theories, we advocate for the rational selection of narrow-spectrum antibiotics and their early and appropriate use, as well as the use of appropriate antidotes, probiotics, and FMT technical support to protect the intestinal barrier, suppress inflammation, and reduce the colonization of harmful microorganisms, thereby facilitating CRC treatment and improving patient quality of life and survival. Intestinal microbiota for the diagnosis and prevention of colorectal cancer

In recent years, the use of intestinal microbiota for the diagnosis and prevention of colorectal cancer has increasingly yielded surprising results as research on intestinal microbiota has intensified. Dietary regulation of intestinal microbiota is used as primary prevention of colorectal cancer, secondary prevention of intestinal microbiota as a diagnostic marker of colorectal cancer, and probiotic, prebiotic, and FMT approaches as tertiary prevention of colorectal cancer. Primary prevention methods—diet

Diet can influence systemic immunological and health states by changing the makeup of the intestinal microbiota, and primary prevention of colorectal cancer should focus on monitoring systemic immune health conditions. A Mediterranean diet rich in nutritional composition and low in fat and protein content meals is excellent for colorectal cancer prevention. A fiber-rich diet is linked to an increase in Treponema species, Butyrivibrio, Prevotella, and Xylanibacter flora, all of which release more short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) (Filippo et al. 2010). SCFAs generated have been shown to improve cell metabolism, induce apoptosis, stabilize the gut microbiota, and suppress tumor cell growth (Yang and Yu 2018). This was also verified by Bingham et al., who discovered that following a vegetarian diet lowered the risk of colorectal cancer significantly (Bingham et al. 2003). In addition, Dahm et al. revealed a negative relationship between dietary fiber consumption and the risk of colorectal cancer (Dahm et al. 2010), whereas Wu et al. discovered a positive relationship between fish and meat diet and the risk of CRC (Wu et al. 2012). Therefore, we believe that a high fiber diet combined with a low protein–fat diet would be beneficial for the primary prevention of CRC.

Secondary prevention methods: diagnosis of intestinal microbiota

CRC secondary prevention should focus on CRC diagnosis. Currently, colonoscopy is the most effective CRC diagnosis, but it has a number of drawbacks, including high costs and poor patient compliance. It is advantageous to use the gut microbiota as a possible non-invasive biomarker for CRC diagnosis. The gut microbiota offers a good theoretical basis for colorectal cancer detection, according to recent research breakthroughs. To begin, microbiota models for early detection are being developed. Flemer et al. found that the quantity of Roseburia, Ruminococus, and Oscilobacter in CRC patients was much higher than in healthy participants, and their distribution sites in the digestive tract were significantly different (Omar Al-Hassi et al. 2018). Meanwhile, Shah et al. discovered that Streptococcus anginosus, Parabacteroides distasonis, and Proteus distasonis had a much greater composition in colorectal cancer patients (Shah et al. 2018). Furthermore, through 16S rRNA sequencing, the flora of F. nucleatum, B. fragilis, and Faecalibacterium prausnitzii might be employed as early diagnostic indicators in patients with colorectal cancer (Wei et al. 2016). Pks island and F. nucleatum were specifically employed for detection, with a specificity and sensitivity of around 70% (Villéger et al. 2018). We included particular risk variables (age, race, and diet) to build a viable model, which would significantly increase the accuracy of early detection. Second, microbiota metabolites can be employed as diagnostic indicators for CRC detection. The metabolites of gut flora have a significant diagnostic value. Bezabeh et al. used nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) to identify SCFAs and amino acids as possible CRC diagnostic indicators (Bezabeh et al. 2009). Phua et al. revealed that intestinal flora metabolites, such as fructose, linoleic acid, and niacin, had great specificity and may be utilized as diagnostic indicators (Phua et al. 2014). Furthermore, lipopolysaccharides (LPS), vitamins, and flora cofactors, as well as the pathways via which they are produced and transformed, can be employed as indicators for CRC diagnosis. Finally, several extra-intestinal proteins and miRNAs can be utilized to identify patients. Microbiota peptides, for example, have a cleaning impact on the gut, and their higher levels influence the rise of IGA in blood, which varies substantially between healthy persons and patients with intestinal inflammation and malignancies, and can be utilized as biomarkers (Montalban-Arques and Scharl 2019). miRNAs, such as miR-515-5p and miR-1226-5p, which are prevalent in patients' feces, might influence CRC development by regulating the proliferation of flora such as F. nucleatum and are therefore regarded diagnostic markers (Toumazi and Constantinou 2020). There are now challenges in separating these markers in feces, as well as limits in these diagnostic indicators, but if the gut microbiota as well as the products can be formed and coordinated as an appropriate model, the diagnostic value will considerably increase.

Tertiary prevention: focus on probiotics, prebiotics, FMT

In the tertiary prevention of colorectal cancer, we should focus on people who have CRC or are at high risk of getting it. It is very critical at this time to halt the growth of CRC and enhance patient survival and quality of life. (1) Probiotics: in tertiary prevention, the role of probiotics is very obvious. Pathogens can be inhibited by the use of preferred probiotics. Particular probiotics can create a specific pH in the bowel and limit the multiplication of pathogens like Clostridium difficile and Staphylococcus aureus, protecting the intestine from further infection (Mills et al. 2018; Piewngam et al. 2018). Second, it enhances the local immunological response. Probiotics like Bifidobacterium bifidum can boost the local immune response by increasing the expression of regulatory T cells (Treg) and intestinal dendritic cells (DC), as well as the release of IL-10 via Toll-like receptors (TLR) (Ghadimi et al. 2012; Chen et al. 2015). Lactobacillus acidophilus decreased intestinal inflammation as well as colorectal cancer in a rat model by lowering MHC class I expression in tumor cells, resulting in improved T cell detection and assault (Chen et al. 2012b). Finally, probiotics can improve intestinal barrier function and restore intestinal integrity. S. boulardii and Bacillus mucilaginous are yeasts that can enhance intestinal barrier function by repairing and boosting tight junction protein expression and creating bioactive lipids with anti-inflammatory and antimicrobiota effects (McFarland 2010). A variety of probiotic genera including Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, and Mucor are able to inhibit the progression of inflammation and restore the intestinal barrier (Fong et al. 2020). (2) Prebiotics: prebiotics, which are largely carbohydrates, act as a medium for the gut flora. It can be found in food, for example, as dietary fiber. It can also be artificially produced lactose, oligogalactose, and other sugars. Prebiotics control intestinal homeostasis and slow CRC advancement by encouraging the establishment of intestinal flora and the creation of metabolites (e.g., SCFA). Prebiotics can boost the quantity of probiotics like bifidomicrobiota and reduce gut pH, delaying the spread of CRC (Markowiak and Śliżewska 2017). Prebiotics stimulate the formation of metabolites, including butyrate, which, as a histone deacetylase inhibitor, can cause apoptosis, decrease tumor cell proliferation, modify DNA methylation, and therefore impede tumor growth (Li and Elsasser 2005; Jan et al. 2002; Leu et al. 2003). Furthermore, prebiotics can indirectly boost the activity of NK cells and T cells, as well as the release of IL-10 cytokines and the local immunological response (Żółkiewicz et al. 2020). (3) FMT: in recent years, research and experimentation on FMT technology have matured, and FMT is progressively becoming another significant approach for treating colorectal cancer. Currently, although FMT is generally used to treat inflammatory bowel illness, mice models show that it has much potential for treating CRC. FMT applied in animal models of CRC can minimize radiation toxicity, significantly increase the number of peripheral immune cells, and raise the survival rate of mice (Cui et al. 2015). FMT-transplanted mice had a more stable intestinal microbiota, a significant increase in the number and activity of immune cells, and a more marked effectiveness in anti-PD-1 immunotherapy (Zhang et al. 2018). Furthermore, in a wild-type mouse model, FMT improved animal health and inhibited tumor development (Rosshart et al. 2017). Based on MPE—providing personalized treatment plans for CRC patients

Molecular pathological epidemiology (MPE) is the application of epidemiological principles and methods based on disease heterogeneity and molecular pathological features to analyze the interaction between exposure factors and disease subtypes, providing scientific strategies for disease prevention, early detection, and treatment, and contributing to precision medicine implementation(Ogino et al. 2019). Based on MPE study, we outline the several genetic pathways that contribute to CRC development, as well as the molecular biomarkers related with diet/lifestyle, genes, immunology, microbiota, and so on. CRC is a very diverse malignancy that is genetically complicated and manifests itself in a unique way(Mima et al. 2021). There are now three major mechanisms that lead to the development of CRC: chromosomal instability (CIN), microsatellite instability (MSI), and the CpG island methylator phenotype (CIMP) (Hughes et al. 2017). CIN is related to mutations and loss of function of critical genes, such as the KRAS oncogene and TP53, adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) tumor suppressor gene, which is characterized by oncogene activation and suppressor gene inactivation (Müller et al. 2016). MSI is mostly caused by aberrant hyper-methylation of mismatch repair (MMR) MLH1 gene or mutations in the MMR gene, which is defined by abnormalities in DNA's repeating microsatellite sequences. MSI is connected with increased BRAF proto-oncogene expression, the RNF43 gene, and suppression of normal apoptosis (Yan et al. 2017). MSI CRC is a better-prognosis subtype that is particularly resistant to chemotherapeutic drugs such as 5-FU (Inamura 2018). CIMP is a type of CRC that is epigenetically unstable. CIMP CRC is more common in women and the elderly and is linked to MSI-related genes or mutations in the BRAF proto-oncogene (Jia et al. 2016). MPE is a relatively new area of study that aims to investigate the association between various environmental exposure variables and disease subtypes to give patients with individualized therapy choices. To provide patients with better therapy choices, we outline the link between diet/lifestyle, immunology, microbiota, and CRC subtypes. (1) Diet: when high-fat meals, such as red meat, are consumed over a long period of time, the incidence of KRAS mutant CRC tumors is much higher than that of KRAS wild-type CRC (Gilsing et al. 2013). Another study found that consuming dietary fiber and unsaturated fatty acids over a long period of time significantly reduced the risk of MRI-high CRC, which may be connected to unsaturated fatty acids' capacity to repair DNA damage (Song et al. 2015). (2) Smoking: prior to the introduction of MPE, there did not appear to be a substantial link between smoking and CRC occurrence. According to current research, smoking significantly increases the incidence of MSI-high, CIMP-high CRC (Hughes et al. 2017). (3) Alcohol: Jayasekra et al. revealed that persistent heavy alcohol use was linked to an elevated incidence of KRAS and BRAF wild-type CRC but not BRAF mutant CRC (Jayasekara et al. 2017). (4) Weight: in the current study, body mass index and waist circumference were found to be positively associated with BRAF mutation, MSI-high, and CIMP-high CRC risk (Hughes et al. 2012). (5) Immunity: CD274 (PD-L1) is an immunosuppressant, and in a CRC patient survival study, it was revealed that related medications such as aspirin had superior therapeutic benefits and higher anti-tumor immune responses in CD274-low CRC (Hamada et al. 2017). (6) Microbiota: Fusobacterium nucleatum aggregation was found in significant concentrations in colorectal cancer tissues (Kostic et al. 2012). Analysis of CRC tissues with MSI, CIMP, and BRAF mutations indicated a substantial concentration of Selenomas and Prevotella (Purcell et al. 2017). In future, we will be able to further explain the pathogenesis of CRC and rationalize the relationship between diet and lifestyle habits, immunity, microbiota, and CRC subtypes, and we will be able to more precisely segment CRC patients and use this as a foundation to provide more personalized diagnosis and treatment plans for CRC patients with different subtypes.

Conclusion

In conclusion, with the rising incidence and lethality of CRC, as well as the limitations of chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and immunotherapy, incorporating intestinal microbes and antibiotics into the aforementioned therapies will significantly improve patient survival and quality of life by establishing a comprehensive system in the three aspects of prevention, diagnosis, and treatment. Although intestinal microorganisms will promote CRC to some extent, the scientific use of antibiotics, including the selection of narrow-spectrum antibiotics, the control of type and duration of antibiotic use, and the rational use of antibiotic antidotes, will allow scientists to retain beneficial flora while removing pathogenic microbiota. In addition, using probiotics can significantly reduce antibiotic resistance, creating a virtuous loop. As the area of intestinal microecology advances, the intestinal microbiota will become an increasingly significant component of CRC prevention, diagnosis, and therapy in future.
