Abstract

Extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography has become the essence of advanced semiconductor manufacturing processes. While enabling smaller feature sizes, EUV lithography imposes increasingly stringent requirements on the comprehensive performance and stochastic defect suppression of photoresist. The widely recognized strategy to minimize these defects is a material that integrates high EUV absorption and energy utilization into a homogeneous system based on molecular building blocks—the ideal formulation for EUV photoresist. However, achieving these integrated characteristics within a single molecule has remained an unresolved challenge. Here, we address all these requirements by polytelluoxane using an organic telluride monomer polymerized via Te─O bonds. This polymeric photoresist, operating through a main chain scission mechanism, demonstrates high-performance positive-tone lithography. Attributed to this ideal formulation, our photoresist achieves a comprehensive 18-nm line width at a dose of 13.1 mJ/cm2 with a line edge roughness of 1.97 nm. We believe that this strategy establishes a framework for the design of next-generation EUV photoresists.

Molecularly homogeneous photoresist achieves ideal formulation with minimal stochastic defects.

INTRODUCTION

As feature sizes approach their physical limits, extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography imposes increasingly stringent demands on the performance of photoresists (1). The 13.5-nm EUV light source relies on an inherently inefficient reflective optical system, which reduces light intensity and necessitates higher sensitivity from the photoresist (2–6). Chemically amplified photoresists (CARs), the most widely used type, use photoacids to catalyze deprotection reactions on the polymer side chains. This catalytic amplification mechanism allows even a small number of EUV photons to induce notable solubility changes, thereby achieving high sensitivity (7). However, the stochastic defects arising from both the photon shot noise caused by their low EUV absorbance and the diffusion of photoacids become increasingly problematic at smaller scales, posing substantial challenges that must be resolved (8–10).

The academic community has reached a consensus on the ultimate formulation required to minimize stochastic defects in photoresists (11). This ideal photoresist must meet the following criteria: (i) a homogeneous system to eliminate defects caused by random distribution, (ii) high EUV absorbance to enhance sensitivity and reduce photon shot noise, (iii) efficient utilization of absorbed EUV energy to confine the reaction volume, and (iv) ultrasmall building blocks to mitigate the effects of feature size.

Achieving these requirements in photoresists remains a substantial challenge. It requires integrating high absorbance and efficient energy utilization into small building blocks with a homogeneous system. The closest approach so far is metal-oxide resists (MORs), which introduce metals with high EUV absorbance (such as Zr, Hf, Zn, and Sn) and use cross-linkable metal-oxide clusters as building blocks to create negative-tone photoresists (12–16). However, recent studies have revealed that their size distribution still induces defects, necessitating extra filtration processes (17). Moreover, the core-shell structure of the clusters, comprising a metal core and ligand shell, hinders their homogeneity. Its derivative, the organometallic photoresist, uses molecular-level components to achieve better homogeneity (18). However, triggering more reactive sites with a single metal center makes it challenging to balance the trade-off between resolution and sensitivity (19, 20). Other attempts have explored multifunctional monomers to incorporate these properties into polymeric systems, but these approaches rely on intricate structural designs and are difficult to achieve both high absorbance and homogeneity (21, 22). Therefore, a feasible approach is urgently required to fully integrate these features into an ideal photoresist formulation.

Here, we address these criteria for photoresists using a single-component polytelluoxane (PTeO) formulation. This formulation is derived from the polymerization of organic telluride monomers via Te─O bonds. Tellurium (Te), the available element with the highest EUV absorption cross section (23), ensures both high sensitivity and photon shot noise suppression. The relatively weak Te─O bond enables in situ cleavage using absorbed EUV energy, achieving efficient positive-tone EUV imaging and minimizing the reaction volume. The [Te─O] building block represents the smallest possible unit, achieving a homogeneous system. While fulfilling these criteria, our photoresist achieves a comprehensive 18-nm line width (LW) at a dose of 13.1 mJ/cm2 with a line-edge roughness (LER) of 1.97 nm. We believe that this straightforward yet highly effective integrated system represents a major breakthrough in the field of photoresists, setting the stage for a new generation of resist design strategies.

RESULTS

EUV absorbance and utilization of PTeO

The structure of PTeO allows for the direct integration of EUV absorbers and responsive bonds into the polymer backbone, thereby achieving high absorbance and efficient utilization of EUV. Linear PTeO exhibits a structure shown in Fig. 1A. These polymers have a backbone composed of alternating Te and O with organic side chains attached to the Te atoms. Te element has an ultrahigh EUV absorption cross section, which is 40.5 times greater than C element and 11.2 times greater than O element (Fig. 1B). Any element with a higher EUV absorption cross section than Te is either a noble gas (Xe and Rn) or radioactive (At). Thus, the strongest EUV absorbers are directly incorporated into the backbone where chain scission will take place in situ after EUV exposure, making PTeO a homogeneous photoresist system with a molecular [Te─O] building block. We synthesized a series of PTeOs with varying side chains (figs. S7 to S13) by modifying the structure of organic telluride (II) monomers (figs. S1 to S6) (24, 25). To date, PTeO is the polymer with the highest atomic number element (Te, Z = 52) incorporated into the backbone.

The high tellurium content of PTeO combined with Te’s higher absorption cross section than other metals (such as recently reported Zr, Hf, Zn, and Sn, shown in Fig. 1C and table S1), gives PTeO the highest EUV absorption than existing photoresists (26). Based on previous studies (27), EUV absorption coefficients (α) could be calculated with absorption cross section and density, aligning well with experiments. Calculated α for PTeOs ranged from 13.2 to 17.0 μm−1, depending on the structure of the organic side chains (table S2). Consequently, the EUV absorbance of PTeOs is nearly three times higher than commercial photoresists such as ZEP520, polymethyl methacrylate, hydrogen silsesquioxane, and typical CARs (Fig. 1D) (23). Moreover, compared to metal-contained photoresists based on Zr or Sn, PTeOs also exhibit enhanced absorbance (28).

The weak Te─O main chain in PTeO facilitates efficient utilization of absorbed EUV energy. Secondary electrons generated by interaction of EUV photons and Te atoms cleave Te─O bonds in situ due to the direct integration of the absorbers into the polymer backbone, improving the overall efficiency of secondary electron generation and utilization. Density functional theory (DFT) simulations of the Te─O bond dissociation in a model molecule (Fig. 1E) show that only 296 kJ/mol is required to cleave the Te─O bond (Fig. 1F and table S3), which is lower than the dissociation energies of C─C and C─O bonds (29) typically found in conventional polymer-based photoresists. The lower bond dissociation energy of Te─O facilitates easier scission of the polymer main chain by secondary electrons, further increasing the EUV efficiency of PTeO. Although calculations indicate a lower activation barrier for Te─C bond cleavage, the formation of Te─Te cross-linked products via Te─C bond cleavage is thermodynamically unfavorable due to the low Te─Te bond energy (<50 kJ/mol). In addition, given the harsh conditions associated with EUV exposure, products are more likely to overcome higher-energy barriers, resulting in the formation of thermodynamically more stable species. Therefore, unlike other metal-carbon–based photoresists that rely on M─C bond cleavage and crosslinking, the dominant mechanism in PTeO involves main chain scission via Te─O bond cleavage.

Because of the unique molecular structure of PTeO, we have successfully met all the criteria for an ideal photoresist mentioned earlier: a fully homogeneous system with high EUV absorption and energy utilization, composed of small building blocks. These properties are crucial for enhancing the comprehensive performance of the photoresist and minimizing stochastic defects.

Mechanism of Te─O main chain scission

During the lithography process, the reduction in molecular weight and the solubility switch resulting from Te─O main chain scission are key mechanisms that enabling PTeO to function as a positive photoresist. Electron beam lithography (EBL) directly provides electrons that interact with the material, similar to EUV, so we used electron beam exposure to simulate an EUV lithography process. Using poly(di-n-butyl)telluoxane (PTeO-diC4) as model polymer, gel permeation chromatography (GPC) was used to detect the reduction of molecular weight during exposure (Fig. 2A). With increasing exposure dose increased, the retention time of the PTeO shifted backward, indicating a substantial decrease in molecular weight (from 33.7 to 0.6 kDa). Raman spectroscopy (Fig. 2B) further elucidated changes in PTeO-diC4 during exposure. Specifically, the peak at 510 cm−1, corresponding to the Te─O bond, gradually diminished, while the peak at 610 cm−1, corresponding to the Te─C bond, exhibited minimal change, confirming that Te─O bond dissociation is the dominant process during exposure. At higher doses, signals attributed to TeO2 appeared in the Raman spectrum, and the Te─C bond peak disappeared (fig. S14), suggesting that Te─C bond dissociation might occur under overexposure conditions. In addition, the reduction of the 280-nm peak in the UV-visible spectrum, corresponding to the Te─O chain, further supported the mechanism of Te─O main chain scission (Fig. 2C).

Methanol, a simple polar solvent, was found to be the best developer to selectively remove the exposed areas during the lithography process. The scission of the Te─O main chain generates oligomers with higher polarity. Mass spectrometry analysis of the developer confirmed the presence of exposure products resulting from Te─O main chain scission (Fig. 2D). In positive ion mode, monomer M1 was identified with proton, methyl, or sodium ion, while dimer M2 was found with proton or methyl. In negative ion mode, monoalkyl telluride was detected (fig. S15), indicating that the Te─C dissociation may also occur during exposure. Subsequently, the methanol developer was treated with a small quantity of NaOMe to alcoholyze the oligomers. The resulting alcoholysis products were characterized by 1H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) and 13C NMR (Fig. 2E and fig. S16). A comparison with independently synthesized di-n-butyl dimethoxy telluride (M3) (fig. S17) revealed that M3 as the primary product, consistent with the alcoholysis products of M1 and M2. The content of M3 was estimated to be approximately 60% based on 1H NMR integration. In addition, distinct butyl signals different from M3 (denoted with *) were observed, likely originating from monoalkyl tellurides produced via Te─C bond dissociation during exposure.

Based on the results of electron beam exposure, we propose an EUV lithography mechanism for PTeO (Fig. 2F). Upon interaction with EUV photons, the Te atom efficiently absorbs these photons, generating secondary electrons that activate the Te─O bonds in the polymer backbone. The dissociation of these bonds produces Te· and O· radicals, which follow an energetically favorable pathway based on our calculation (Fig. 1F). These radicals lead subsequently to scission, resulting in the formation of oligomers with higher polarity, thereby triggering a solubility switch. The contrast in solubility between the unexposed polymer and the exposed oligomers enables a positive-tone patterning of micro features.

Performance of PTeO in EUV lithography

The homogeneous PTeO system, on one hand, reduces photon stochastic defects due to its high EUV absorbance and, on the other hand, minimizes chemical stochastic defects through the in situ utilization of EUV and small building blocks. As a result, PTeO demonstrates excellent sensitivity, resolution, and LER in EUV lithography. We conducted EUV exposure experiments at the Shanghai Synchrotron Radiation Facility (SSRF) using their beamline to do interference lithography to investigate the lithographic performance of PTeO.

The high EUV absorbance of Te atoms allows PTeO to be patterned at remarkably low doses, demonstrating high sensitivity in EUV lithography. The thickness-dose curve for PTeOs (figs. S18 and S19) showed that the exposed areas could be completely removed at doses below 20 mJ/cm2. For periodic line patterns, poly(phenyl-n-propyl)telluoxane (PTeO-PhC3) exhibited the highest sensitivity, achieving sparse lines with an LW of 24 nm at a dose of 9.1 mJ/cm2 [Fig. 3A(a)]. For finer 19-nm and 18-nm LW, the required exposure doses were only 10.9 and 13.1 mJ/cm2 with an LER of 3.31 and 1.97 nm, respectively [Fig. 3A(b) and (c), and fig. S20].

The formulation of PTeO, which satisfies the four criteria outlined above, enables low LER and good resolution by reducing stochastic defects. We obtained features with reduced LER in poly(phenyl-ethyl)telluoxane (PTeO-PhC2) [Fig. 3B, (a) to (c), and figs. S21 and S22], successfully fabricating the thinnest lines of 16 nm with an LER of 1.75 nm. For dense patterns, we achieved features measuring half pitch (HP) of 30, 25, and 20 nm with an LER of 2.62, 1.96, and 2.15 nm, respectively [Fig. 3C, (a) to (c), and fig. S23].

The Te─O main chain provides the necessary chemical mechanism for effective EUV lithography, while the diverse side chains offer tunability to meet specific requirements in lithographic process. We evaluated the performance of six distinct PTeOs with varying side chains in EUV lithography. For instance, poly(phenyl-n-butyl)telluoxane (PTeO-PhC4) achieved the best resolution in dense pattern with a minimum HP of 18 nm [Fig. 3D, (a) to (d)]. Further EUV lithography experiments were conducted under various development conditions (figs. S24 and S25) and with PTeOs lacking phenyl groups (fig. S26). These results indicate that the side-chain structures can be modified to achieve targeted micro features. Optimal LER performance is achieved through a careful balance between molecular rigidity—minimizing thermally induced line-edge fluctuations—and solubility contrast upon EUV exposure, both of which are strongly influenced by side-chain design (fig. S27).

So far we have successfully used PTeO to develop an EUV photoresist with excellent performance that efficiently reduces stochastic defects through its ideal formulation. By modulating the side chains of PTeO, we can tailor the material for various processing requirements, facilitating the production of desired micro features. This adaptability underscores the potential for customizing polymer formulations to meet specific application needs in industrial contexts.

Performance of PTeO in EBL

The Te─O main chain scission mechanism is versatile and applicable to various lithographic techniques, such as ultraviolet and EBL. We applied various electron doses across different areas (Fig. 4A), and atomic force microscopy (AFM) was used to characterize the post-development morphology of PTeO (Fig. 4, B to D). We plotted dose-thickness curves for the six synthesized PTeOs (Fig. 4E). As the dose increased, the thickness of the remaining photoresist decreased until complete removal was achieved. Generally, the dose to clear (D0) for PTeO ranged from 120 to 350 μC/cm2, with contrast values between 1.0 and 1.5.

We observed that PTeO maintained consistent sensitivity and contrast across a broad molecular weight range (Fig. 4F and figs. S28 and S29). This consistency is likely attributed to the main chain scission mechanism, which requires a similar number of Te─O bonds to dissociate PTeO into small molecules, regardless of the molecular weight. Consequently, the sensitivity of PTeO remains unaffected by fluctuations in molecular weight, indicating a potential of the stability in both lithography and storage of PTeO.

The development properties of PTeO can be controlled using a simple solvent mixture of methanol and water. As the water content increases, the dose required for complete development also rises. This is due to the increased polarity of the developer, which reduces the solubility of incompletely cleaved products, necessitating a higher dose to fully cleave PTeO into small molecules. This phenomenon was observed in both symmetric PTeO-diC4 (Fig. 4G) and asymmetric PTeO-PhC4 (fig. S30). Although higher water content requires a higher D0, it improves control over LW and reduces photoresist swelling during development. We successfully achieved periodic line patterns characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Fig. 4, H to J, and figs. S31 to S33). Notably, as the water content in the developer increased, the LW also increased. This is because the partially exposed area is less soluble in a more polar developer. All conditions exhibited a low LER around 2 nm (fig. S34) attributed to the ideal formulation. Cross section SEM results (Fig. 4K and figs. S35 and S36) confirmed that the lithographic lines exhibited good profile.

A variety of complex patterns were successfully fabricated using EBL with PTeO-PhC4, demonstrating the photoresist’s versatility. Figure 4 (I and M) illustrates the precise patterning of simple geometric shapes, English letters, and Chinese characters. Figure 4 (N and O) further showcases the detailed fabrication of periodic table, featuring element symbols and their corresponding Chinese characters, respectively.

We demonstrated the performance of PTeO in EBL, highlighting their robustness across different molecular weights and the adjustable performance with various mixtures of developer. In addition to EUV lithography and EBL, PTeO also exhibited commendable performance in UV (I-line and 254 nm) lithography (figs. S37 to S40), further underscoring the universality of PTeO photoresists. Moreover, we have developed a tellurium recovery strategy to mitigate the potential high cost and toxicity associated with Te-based materials (fig. S41). The comprehensive advantages of PTeO under various exposure sources emphasize its substantial potential for advancing the next generation of photoresists.

DISCUSSION

With the unique molecular structure of PTeO, we have successfully achieved an ideal photoresist formulation. The highly absorptive Te elements are compactly integrated into a molecularly homogeneous polymer system via weak Te─O bonds, enabling excellent positive-tone lithographic performance through a main chain scission mechanism. We demonstrate a low LER of 1.97 nm with an LW of 18 nm at a dose of 13.1 mJ/cm2. Futhermore, PTeO offers a notably simpler structure and synthesis method compared to conventional CARs or MORs while eliminating the need for baking steps. We believe that these advantages will facilitate further validation of this novel photoresist and lead the design strategies for the next generation of lithographic materials.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

General methods

Reagents

Tellurium powder and sodium borohydride were purchased from Aladdin Chemical Company, China. Hydrogen peroxide 30% aqueous solution was purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd. N-butyl bromide, n-propyl bromide, ethyl iodide, and diphenyl ditelluride were purchased from Meryer Technologies Co. Ltd. Organic solvents such as chloroform, dichloromethane, ethyl acetate, hexane, methanol, and tetrahydrofuran (THF) used in this work were purchased from Beijing Chemical Reagent Company, China. Magnesium sulfate and sodium methoxide were purchased from Shanghai Titan Scientific Co. Ltd.

Apparatus

The NMR spectra were recorded by BRUKER ASCENDTM 400 spectrometer. The mass spectra were obtained by Bruker Daltonic 1200HPLC. Scanning electron microscope images were obtained through the ZEISS GeminiSEM 300. Atomic force microscope results were obtained through the Bruker ICON. The UV-vis–near-infrared absorption was characterized by a UV-2450-visible spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan). The x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy was obtained on a PHI Quantera scanning x-ray microprobe. Raman spectra were obtained through a Horiba Evolution spectrometer. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) was conducted in a TA Discovery250 instrument. GPC was conducted in an Agilent1260. EUV lithography was done at SSRF through a soft x-ray interference lithograph beamline. EBL was done with Raith150 Two. UV exposures were done in contact mode with a mask placed on top of a coated wafer using a light box with a mercury arc lamp filtered to give 254-nm light.

General procedures for the syntheses of raw materials

Synthesis of dialkyl telluride

NaHTe was prepared in situ by the reaction of tellurium powder (2.55 g, 20.0 mmol) and sodium borohydride (7.57 g, 200 mmol) at 50°C in deionized water (40 ml) under the atmosphere of nitrogen. After stirring for 30 min, the solution remained clear and light purple. A solution of alkyl bromide/iodide (40.0 mmol) in THF (40 ml) was added slowly at 0°C. Then, the mixture was stirred at 50°C for 3 hours, and the reaction was complete. The solution was extracted with ethyl acetate (40 ml) for three times. The organic layers were combined and dried with anhydrous sodium sulfate, and the solvent was removed by rotatory evaporation. The crude product was purified by column chromatography with 10% ethyl acetate/hexane. Dialkyl telluride was obtained as a yellow liquid. Each specific molecular synthesis procedure can be found in Supplementary Text.

Synthesis of aryl-alkyl telluride

Sodium aryltelluride (NaTeAr) was prepared in situ by the reaction of di-aryl ditelluride (2.00 mmol) and sodium borohydride (0.757 g, 20 mmol) in a mixture of deionized water (20 ml) and THF (10 ml) under the atmosphere of nitrogen. After stirring for 15 min, the solution remained clear and light yellow. A solution of alkyl bromide/iodide (4.00 mmol) in THF (10 ml) was added. Then, the mixture was stirred at 50°C for 3 hours, and the reaction was complete. The solution was extracted with ethyl acetate (20 ml) for three times. The organic layers were combined and dried with anhydrous sodium sulfate, and the solvent was removed by rotatory evaporation. The crude product was purified by column chromatography with 10% ethyl acetate/hexane. Aryl-alkyl telluride was obtained as a yellow liquid. Each specific molecular synthesis procedure can be found in Supplementary Text.

Synthesis of PTeO

A 0.500 g of organic telluride(II) was dissolved in 2 ml of chloroform. To this solution, 2 ml of H2O2 (5 mol/liter) was added dropwise at 0°C. The above solution was stirred for 48 hours at room temperature. When the reaction was done, a viscous organic layer was obtained. The mixture was extracted with 10 ml of chloroform and washed with 10 ml of H2O. Then, the organic layer was collected and dropped into 100 ml of methanol slowly. After filtration and washing with methanol, PTeO was obtained as a white powder. Each specific molecular synthesis procedure can be found in Supplementary Text.

Synthesis of M3

Di-n-butyl telluride (100 mg, 0.414 mmol) was dissolved in 2 ml of diethyl ether. Then tetrachlorotellurium (59.0 mg, 0.220 mmol) was added, resulting in the immediate precipitation of a black solid. After filtration, 50 μl of NaOMe (0.1 mol/liter) in methanol was added. The reaction completed within 10 min. A white precipitation was filtered, and the remaining solvent was removed by rotatory evaporation. The M3 was obtained as a white solid. 1H NMR [400 MHz, CDCl3, parts per million (ppm)]: δ 3.38 (He, s, 6H), 2.74(Ha, t, 4H), 1.86(Hb, p, 4H), 1.44(Hc, h, 4H), 0.94(Hd, t, 6H). 13C NMR (101 MHz, CDCl3, ppm): δ 52.94(Ce), 37.97(Ca), 27.07(Cb), 24.83(Cc), 13.61(Cd).

Recycling strategy of Te-containing species

An appropriate amount of reducing agent (such as Vitamin C or sodium borohydride) was added to the developer solution, followed by the addition of water and dichloromethane for extraction. After separating and discarding the aqueous phase, the organic phase was dried over anhydrous MgSO4. The organic solvent was removed by rotary evaporation to obtain an orange-yellow liquid, which is a mixture of dialkyl tellurides and dialkyl ditellurides. A suitable amount of dichloromethane was added to this mixture, followed by the addition of hydrogen peroxide to initiate polymerization, yielding polytelluoxane (PTeO).

General procedures for the characterization and lithography

DSC experiments of PTeOs

Differential scanning calorimetry was conducted using a TA Discovery250 instrument. The procedure segments were described as follows:

For PTeO-diCn (n = 2 to 4): (i) equilibrate at 25.00°C; (ii) ramp 20.00°C/min to 120.00°C; (iii) isothermal for 5.00 min; (iv) ramp 10.00°C/min to −90.00°C; (v) ramp 10.00°C/min to 140.00°C.

For PTeO-PhCn (n = 2 to 4): (i) equilibrate at 25.00°C; (ii) ramp 20.00°C/min to 90.00°C; (iii) isothermal for 5.00 min; (iv) ramp 10.00°C/min to −90.00°C; (v) ramp 10.00°C/min to 150.00°C.

General procedures of EUV lithography

After ultrasonic cleaning of a 2-inch silicon wafer with isopropanol for 10 min, the wafer was baked at 110°C for another 10 min. When the wafer cooled down, the PTeO film was obtained by spin coating. The concentration of PTeO in solvent could be variable depending on the target thickness. In general, chloroform was used as the solvent, and the concentration was 8 mg/ml to obtain a film of ~20 nm. The spin rate was 3000 rpm. After spin coating, the film was air-dried at room temperature (25°C) for 1 min. After exposed in EUV, the development was done using a mixture of water and methanol. The content of water varied from 0 to 20%. The wafer was dried under N2 flow.

General procedures of EBL

After ultrasonic cleaning of a 1 cm–by–1 cm silicon wafer with isopropanol for 10 min, the wafer was baked at 110°C for another 10 min. When the wafer cooled down, PTeO film was obtained by spin coating. The concentration of PTeO in solvent could be variable depending on the target thickness. In general, chloroform was used as the solvent, and the concentration was 12 mg/ml to obtain a film of ~30 nm. After spin coating, the film was air-dried at room temperature (25°C) for 1 min. After exposed in EUV, the development was done using a mixture of water and methanol. The content of water varied from 0 to 20%. The wafer was dried under N2 flow.

General procedures of UV Lithography

After ultrasonic cleaning of a 1 cm–by–1 cm silicon wafer with isopropanol for 10 min, the wafer was baked at 110°C for another 10 min. When the wafer cooled down, PTeO film was obtained by spin coating. The concentration of PTeO in solvent could be variable depending on the target thickness. Generally, chloroform was used as the solvent, and the concentration was 30 mg/ml to obtain a film of ~80 nm. After spin coating, the film was air-dried at room temperature for 1 min. After spin coating, the film was air-dried at room temperature (25°C) for 1 min. After being exposed in EUV, the development was done using a mixture of water and methanol. The content of water varied from 0 to 20%. The wafer was dried under N2 flow.

General procedures for theoretical calculations

EUV absorption

The EUV linear absorption coefficient α of a compound could be calculated by the formula below

α=ρMr∑ixiσi

where ρ is the density of the compound, Mr is the molecular weight (or molecular weight of a repeat unit for polymers), xi is the amount of the ith element in the molecular formula, σi is the atomic absorption cross section of each element.

DFT simulation of Te─O dissociation

All DFT calculations were implemented using the Gaussian 16 software version B.01. The DFT functional of B3LYP associated with the Grimme empirical dispersion correction GD3(BJ) was used for geometry optimization of all species. The def2-SVP basis set was used for all elements. To confirm that the optimized structure is a local minimum with no imaginary frequency and to acquire the thermal correction to the Gibbs-free energy, frequency calculations were carried out at the same level. Based on the optimized structure, a single-point energy calculation was performed with M06-2X functional with the Grimme empirical dispersion correction GD3, and ma-def2-TZVPP basis set (30) was used in this step. The presented Gibbs-free energy refers to the sum of the single-point energy (M06-2X-D3/ma-def2-TZVPP level) and the thermal correction to Gibbs-free energy [B3LYP-D3(BJ)/def2-SVP level].

LER calculation

The unbiased LER was calculated by SEM-Measured Image Lines Estimator (31). Edge fit function was “polynomial,” and SEM model was “Zeiss.” Other parameters in edge detection were defaulted. “Palasantzas2” was chosen as power spectral density model.
