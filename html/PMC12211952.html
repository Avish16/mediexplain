Abstract

Objective

To explore the perspectives and experiences of nurses providing end-of-life care in the intensive care units.

Methods

This study employed a descriptive phenomenological approach, conducting semi-structured interviews with 13 intensive care units’ nurses from November 2023 to February 2024. The interviews were audio-recorded, transcribed verbatim, and analyzed using Colaizzi analysis to categorize and organize the data.

Results

We categorized the findings into four themes: (1) Institutional constraints in end-of-life care, (2) Cultural-cognitive conflicts, (3) Communication breakdown and trust crisis, (4) Healing and resilience.

Conclusion

This study provides an in-depth analysis of the key factors influencing the implementation of end-of-life care in intensive care units within the Chinese cultural context, highlighting the multidimensional challenges encountered. By meticulously analyzing barriers and facilitators, the study offers insights for effective resource utilization (division of labor within the medical team, balancing the intensity of medical interventions with patients’ comfort needs) and targeted strategy development. Optimizing intensive care unit environments, enhancing cultural sensitivity education, promoting team collaboration, and providing emotional support for nurses are essential pathways to improving the effectiveness of end-of-life care in the intensive care units.

Keywords: Intensive care units, End-of-life care, Qualitative research

Background

The quality of death for patients in the intensive care unit (ICU) is often suboptimal. A study conducted across 84 countries found that ICU mortality rates range from 9.3–26.2% [1], with rates exceeding 35.5% during the COVID-19 pandemic [2]. Before death, ICU patients frequently undergo invasive treatments such as tracheostomy, central venous catheterization, and arterial catheterization. A retrospective study in Germany indicated that more than 60% of patients had at least one life-sustaining treatment continued until death [3]. These invasive interventions can cause additional suffering for patients and emotional distress for their families [4]. In Wen’s study [4], within the 24 months following the loss of a loved one, poorer quality of death was associated with increased depressive symptoms in relatives and a decline in health-related quality of life. Increasing evidence shows [5–7] that implementing end-of-life care can improve the quality of death for patients and enhance family satisfaction. The National Library of Medicine defines end-of-life care as [8] “medical, psychological, and spiritual support provided by healthcare professionals and volunteers to patients with terminal illnesses, aiming to help dying individuals attain peace, comfort, and dignity.”

The implementation of end-of-life care continues to face numerous obstacles, which vary across different regions. In Middle Eastern countries [9], healthcare professionals lack clear guidance and support regarding do-not-resuscitate (DNR) decisions and ethical dilemmas, and differing understandings of end-of-life care between families and ICU staff are significant barriers in the end-of-life care process. A study conducted in the UK identified major obstacles as including staff shortages, patient complexity, and inadequate infrastructure [10]. Recent qualitative research in the United States highlighted that communication with families and patients about end-of-life care is the most prominent challenge faced by physicians [11].

China’s cultural and healthcare environments critically shape end-of-life care. Confucian filial piety grants families absolute authority in medical decision-making [12], driving the continuation of futile treatments to avoid the stigma of “unfiliality” and demanding concealment of terminal diagnoses from patients. Compounded by the societal taboo of death as a “curse,” discussions about prognosis are delayed, while in ICUs, family-dominated decisions and communication barriers fuel ethical-clinical tensions [13]. Nurses face dual challenges: mediating families’ demands for aggressive interventions while advocating for patient dignity—a struggle intensified by the absence of protocols to harmonize cultural norms with end-of-life care evidence. Understanding their experiences is essential to overcoming systemic barriers and optimizing care. While existing studies have identified structural barriers to end-of-life care [14], this study’s qualitative exploration of nurses’ experiences in navigating cultural-ethical dilemmas provides unique and indispensable evidence for developing context-specific practices aligned with China’s sociocultural realities. Therefore, this study aims to phenomenologically explore the essential experiences of ICU nurses in providing end-of-life care, with further examination of how these experiences reveal both barriers and facilitators in clinical practice.

Methods

Study design

This study employed a descriptive phenomenological approach combined with Colaizzi analysis [15]. The study adhered to the Consolidated Criteria for Reporting Qualitative Research (COREQ) guidelines for qualitative research reporting [16].

Participants

Our data collection process utilized shift schedules provided by head nurses, with invitations distributed via internal hospital emails. The first participant was interviewed in a quiet conference room during non-working hours, followed by convenience sampling for subsequent participants. ICU nurses were conveniently sampled from a 63-bed adult ICU (with 7 single rooms) at Zhongnan Hospital of Wuhan University for inclusion in the study. The study period was from November 2023 to February 2024. This study employed convenience sampling and adhered to the following inclusion criteria: (1) Registered nurse; (2) Age > 18 years; (3) At least 2 years of experience in ICU nursing or management; (3) Participants were required to work both day and night shifts during the study period to minimize cognitive bias. Exclusion criteria were: (1) Nurses on leave; (2) Nurses without experience in providing end-of-life care. The sample size was guided by the principle of thematic saturation in qualitative research. A total of 13 nurses were included in the study, with their general characteristics detailed in Table 1. Notably, the majority (n = 11, 84.6%) had provided end-of-life care to a minimum of 10 terminally ill patients prior to this study.

Research methods

This phenomenological study employed semi-structured interviews to explore nurses’ experiences.

Development of the interview guide

Literature [17–18] and consultations with two clinical nursing experts (with over 10 years of practical experience in ICU end-of-life care) and two qualitative research methodologists specializing in narrative analysis and cross-cultural healthcare communication. Following pilot interviews with two ICU nurses (whose data were excluded from final analysis), the guide was systematically revised to: (a) restructure question sequencing from general to specific topics for improved narrative flow (e.g.,moving from general workflow questions to specific end-of-life care challenges), (b) optimize follow-up prompts to elicit more detailed responses (adding probes like ‘Can you provide examples’). These modifications, implemented through research team consensus, aimed to minimize participant discomfort and improve data richness, with all researchers uniformly applying the finalized version. The finalized interview guide is presented in Table 2.

Data collection methods

All three researchers have received interview training and have interview experience. A descriptive qualitative study using face-to-face semi-structured interviews was conducted with 13 clinical nurses. Thematic saturation was reached by the 11th interview, as no new meaningful patterns emerged in subsequent data collection. Prior to interviews, researchers established quiet, private environments in ward/outpatient areas (neutrally arranged, devoid of religious symbols) and implemented synchronized dual-device recording (digital voice recorder and smartphone). Interviewers explicitly declared neutrality (no conflicts of interest, non-judgmental listening). The interviews, guided by a semi-structured protocol and lasting 30–45 min, employed multiple trust-building techniques to elicit authentic responses: establishing rapport through pre-interview dialogues, utilizing open-ended questions with strategic pauses, and ensuring a confidential environment where participants could pause the session at any time - all while carefully avoiding leading interventions. Post-interview, dual coding, discrepancy negotiations (the third researcher makes the judgment), and participant feedback loops systematically addressed analytical biases. This rigorous process ensured methodological transparency, balancing environmental control, reflexive practice, and iterative validation across data collection and analysis phases.

Data analysis methods

The interview content was transcribed into Nvivo 12.0 software and analyzed using Colaizzi analysis methods [15]. Unclear statements were clarified through paraphrasing, with non-verbal cues (body language, tone) documented as supplementary data. Within 24 h of completing the interviews, the transcripts were prepared and sent to the participants for verification. Once confirmed by the participants, the data analysis commenced. Two researchers independently coded the interview data. They first carefully read the transcribed interviews and then proceeded with data analysis, identifying and coding significant statements. Any discrepancies were resolved by a third researcher (an independent qualitative methodologist in end-of-life care research, uninvolved in data collection), leading to consensus. Additionally, two independent external experts with extensive qualitative research experience (free from conflicts of interest) conducted a blinded review of 20% of the interview transcripts and thematic coding to ensure analytical rigor. Data collection and analysis occurred simultaneously, with ongoing revisions and categorization of existing codes to incorporate new information.

Data credibility

The interviewers received training in qualitative research methods and have prior interview experience. In the two-month pre-interview phase, the research team attended daily clinical handovers and conducted observational shadowing of nursing shifts to acclimate to ICU operations. Concurrent informal dialogues addressing non-study themes (e.g., staffing logistics, occupational stressors) were utilized to cultivate a naturalistic interaction environment. After coding the interview data, the codes and interpretations were reviewed with the participants to ensure accuracy and credibility. To verify the confirmability of the data, we cross-checked the findings with the original data and interview notes. For dependability, we documented the data with specific and precise direct quotations from the participants and had the data reviewed by personnel outside the research team. To ensure reliability, we presented detailed information to a quality advisor, including the specifics of the data, the interview process, transcription, and analysis. All research team members declared no conflicts of interest related to the research topic.

Ethics

This study has been approved by the Ethics Committee of Zhongnan Hospital of Wuhan University (ethics number: 2019104). All participants provided written informed consent and voluntarily agreed to participate in this study. During the research, confidentiality was ensured by protecting participants’ information and using numerical identifiers to maintain their anonymity. The research data were stored on a computer with encrypted files to secure the information.

Results

We consolidated the results into four themes: (1) Institutional constraints in end-of-life care, (2) Cultural-cognitive conflicts, (3) Communication breakdown and trust crisis, (4) Healing and resilience. The main themes and sub-themes are presented in Fig. 1.

Theme 1: institutional constraints in end-of-life care

The limited space in ICU deprives intimacy at life’s end

The design of ICUs inherently undermines privacy through cramped spaces (P1: “No private environment”) and technological intrusions that disrupt dignified end-of-life rituals, manifesting the alienation of farewell ceremonies within the mathematized space of intensive care. Researcher observations reveal how “farewells degenerate into spectator events,” exposing the fundamental tension between clinical environments and the intimate nature of death. Nurses develop compensatory strategies (P3: “Creating private space improves family-patient communication”), improvising transient moments of intimacy within institutional rigidities. By temporarily suspending ICU’s “scientific natural attitude,” they reconstruct intersubjective spaces for mourning—a fragile restoration of the pre-reflective lifeworld. This psychological compensation mechanism has itself become an essential professional practice for sustaining care within anti-intimate architectures.

Sensory violence in ICU settings

The constant technological alarms and human-generated noise in ICU environments collectively create “dehumanizing temporal markers,” with nine participants reporting: “(The ICU setting) is extremely noisy and causes significant patient discomfort.” Within this soundscape, nurses assume dual roles as both passive recipients and active participants. The abrupt triggering of medical equipment alarms disrupts the natural flow of temporal perception while simultaneously alerting nurses to patients’ distress states. This dialectic reveals the ICU as a Husserlian “field of sense-bestowal”, where the crisis of technological temporality must be constantly reconciled with the lifeworld’s primordial temporal structures.

Care fragmentation in end-of-life care

The tension between holistic patient care and nursing shortages compels nurses to prioritize technical tasks over compassionate presence at life’s end. As one nurse describes, “One nurse to multiple patients prevents ideal care.” This reduction transforms nursing practice into mechanical monitoring - “Just keeping parameters normal” - with another lamenting, “We’re data monitors now.” Such technological reductionism converts caregivers into mere vital sign observers, exemplifying what Husserl termed the naturalization of consciousness. Even fundamental human rituals become compromised, as expressed in the poignant observation: “Families can’t properly say farewell here.” This reveals how medicalized consciousness systematically erases the lifeworld’s mourning practices, demonstrating the colonization of intersubjective space by objectifying scientific rationality. The sedimentation of technical routines obscures nursing’s essential intersubjective meaning, exposing the crisis when institutional demands override the phenomenological reality of care.

Theme 2: cultural-cognitive conflicts

Death as a cultural taboo

At the cognitive level, Chinese society has developed a unique “linguistic- ritual-emotional” taboo system regarding death. Linguistically, an elaborate euphemistic lexicon has emerged (P5: “We use terms like ‘passed away’ or ‘gone’ instead of ‘death’ when speaking with families”), which essentially represents a discursive practice rooted in the Confucian philosophy of “While you do not know life, how can you know about death?” Ritually, the insistence on “bodily integrity” (P2: “Families demand their [patients’] bodies remain intact”) stems from traditional bodily symbolism, where fluctuations in medical conditions are often interpreted as signs of “ancestral protection.” Emotionally, family members employ prolonged treatment measures to postpone the onset of grief—a phenomenon that stands in stark contrast to cultures practicing “advance care planning.” This tripartite system collectively functions as a cultural immune mechanism against death anxiety.

Conflicts in medical practice

Clinical practice reveals a distinctive “resuscitation theater” phenomenon, where families insist on maintaining vital signs to preserve the appearance of life. This practice, reported by five participants, essentially employs life-support technology to fulfill cultural expectations of an “auspicious death.” Medical monitors’ numerical displays become emotional anchors for grieving relatives. Notably, six participants documented requests to prolong life until culturally significant dates (e.g., “after Spring Festival” or “the elder’s birthday”), demonstrating how traditional temporal values directly influence medical decisions. The ICU thus transforms into a modern stage for performing filial piety, where extended resuscitation assumes ritual significance beyond medical purpose. This phenomenon highlights the complex interplay between medical technology and deep-rooted cultural beliefs in end-of-life care.

Psychological and cultural barriers in end-of-life medical decision-making

The drive to survive manifests as both biological instinct (P4: “Patients often have a strong will to live”) and cultural construct. Individually expressed through life-extension efforts, this impulse is amplified by death-avoidance norms. When medical realities challenge survival narratives, patients experience cognitive dissonance (P7: “They interpret doctors’ advice as wanting them to die”), distorting end-of-life discussions into perceived abandonment. Clinicians’ recommendations become subconsciously viewed as existential threats, hindering rational dialogue. This survival imperative often combines with familial duty (P10: “Patients persist for their family”), creating entrenched resistance to end-of-life conversations and forming a psychological barrier to medically sound decisions. The resultant psychological barrier operates through what Husserl termed “passive synthesis,” where rational medical judgment becomes phenomenologically inaccessible beneath these pre-reflective meaning-structures.

Theme 3: communication breakdown and trust crisis

Dual dilemmas in end-of-life care communication

Effective end-of-life communication faces dual barriers: deficient emotional connection and eroded team trust. Healthcare providers often default to mechanical updates when unable to establish rapport (P8: “I can only report vital signs”), while traditional medical training fails to equip them for existential distress (P5: “I feel at a loss”). Compromised teamwork (P7: “deteriorating collaboration”) exacerbates this, creating a vicious cycle where defensive practices further drain emotional capacity for meaningful communication.

Medical disputes in end-of-life decision making

Family members’ cognitive patterns are typically shaped by two key factors: information asymmetry and prior experiences with medical disputes. This particular cognitive framework predisposes families to interpret medical actions through an economic lens, as articulated by four respondents: “Families may believe we’re prolonging treatment to increase hospital revenue” and “They think you’re deceiving them.” These accounts demonstrate a Husserlian intersubjective trust crisis where healthcare’s natural attitude is displaced by commercial intentionality through passive synthesis, transforming therapeutic alliances into relationships of mistrust.

Theme 4: healing and resilience

The therapeutic power of sensory connections

Sensory interventions provide powerful psychological support through two key pathways. Auditory stimulation using familiar sounds (P3: “playing the patient’s favorite music or family recordings”) creates emotional continuity, triggering memory centers to maintain connection when speech fails. Simultaneously, therapeutic touch (P13: “holding hands or gentle stroking”) activates primal comfort mechanisms, fostering security through physical contact. These combined approaches effectively reconstruct attachment bonds for nonverbal patients, offering comfort through: (1) Auditory familiarity that maintains presence, and (2) Tactile reassurance that conveys love. By guiding families to combine these sensory methods while avoiding distress, healthcare providers create meaningful non-verbal communication that significantly reduces terminal anxiety.

The psychological support of spiritual belief

Spiritual beliefs provide crucial psychological support through symbolic and ritual dimensions. Symbolically, religious objects like amulets (P12: “Family members might hang religious amulets at the patient’s bedside.“) act as transitional items, creating sacred spaces that combat death anxiety while giving families comforting proxy actions. Ritually, prayer (P7: “Relatives turn to prayer for blessings.“) serves as primary control behavior, restoring agency when medical options are exhausted. These dual mechanisms: (1) Offer tangible comfort through sacred symbols, and (2) Provide emotional regulation through structured rituals. Healthcare teams can leverage this system by respectfully accommodating spiritual practices, which reduces family distress and facilitates better medical communication.

Bidirectional emotional bonding

Healthcare providers and patients/families form mutually therapeutic connections. Caregivers intentionally form profound attachments that extend beyond professional obligations (P11: “I feel very close to this person”), where the heightened vulnerability to grief paradoxically confers existential meaning to their work. This intersubjective connection finds its reciprocal validation in familial expressions of gratitude (P4: “Her daughter’s hug gave me unprecedented worth”), establishing an emotional dialectic that fulfills fundamental human needs. When medicine reaches its limits at life’s end, human connection becomes the essential anchor. This reflects Husserl’s intentional orientation, where care relationships fulfill deep psychological needs, creating an irreducible bond beyond clinical objectivity.

Self-regulation strategies

In the absence of systematic psychological support, healthcare workers spontaneously develop adaptive coping mechanisms. First, they establish transitional rituals between work and personal life through “daily ritualized defenses” (P3: “I go out for ice cream or dessert with colleagues to gradually forget about it”), facilitating effective psychological transition. Second, they rely on an “implicit peer support network”—given their shared professional context and to avoid external misunderstandings, they prefer discussing emotional issues with colleagues (P5: “Compared to others, I’d rather talk to my coworkers”). Lastly, they engage in meaning reconstruction, transforming special moments of gratitude from families into reserves of professional value, which help buffer against subsequent work-related frustrations.

Disscusion

This study, from the perspective of nurses, describes the multiple challenges faced in implementing end-of-life care in the ICU. These challenges include the loss of privacy and noisy environment due to the unique characteristics of the ICU, the erosion of trust and staff shortages in team collaboration, cultural taboos surrounding death affecting patient and family acceptance, and decision-making difficulties stemming from patients’ strong will to live and spiritual beliefs. Additionally, issues such as nurses’ hesitation in communication, family decision-making dilemmas, medical dispute risks, and emotional burdens collectively contribute to the complex factors influencing the effectiveness of end-of-life care.

ICU environments pose structural barriers to dignified end-of-life care, with noise pollution and scarce private spaces (often reserved for isolation needs) [19] complicating familial closure. Nurses adapt through provisional solutions: bed curtains demarcate symbolic privacy zones [20], minimized alarms reduce sensory stress, and complementary therapies (music/aromatherapy) ease patient distress [21]. These improvised solutions represent attempts to reconstruct patients’ and families’ Lebenswelt (lifeworld) - a concept underscored by Husserl’s “crisis of the sciences,” where technological dominance systematically overrides fundamental human needs for intersubjective connection. China’s ICUs face systemic constraints [22]—limited staffing and underdeveloped interdisciplinary collaboration (e.g., lacking chaplains/social workers)—forcing nurses to assume roles beyond clinical care. Cultivating specialized end-of-life care teams emerges as a priority to address unmet psychosocial-spiritual needs within resource-constrained critical care systems.

The study uncovers how Chinese death taboos manifest as hermeneutic obstacles to end-of-life care acceptance. Families navigating end-of-life decisions for terminally ill loved ones face culturally embedded dilemmas shaped by death taboos, fragmented health literacy, and filial piety pressures [14, 23, 24]. Participants’ reports of families refusing to acknowledge terminal diagnoses exemplify Husserl’s concept of sedimentation, where Confucian and Buddhist traditions form passive syntheses that predetermine responses to mortality. Rooted in Chinese norms where death remains a taboo topic, families often equate end-of-life care with cultural betrayal while struggling to reconcile aggressive life-prolongation (driven by societal “filial piety” expectations) with medical futility [14]. Information asymmetry exacerbates misconceptions, fueling denial of prognosis or pursuit of non-traditional therapies despite irreversible conditions. Nurses observed these contradictions manifest as refusal to acknowledge terminal status, unrealistic hope for miracles, or therapeutic overreach—behaviors amplifying patient suffering and familial distress. Family meetings, as an effective approach, can provide family members with detailed information about the patient and more accurately clarify the needs of both the patient and the family. Existing research indicates that this can alleviate caregiver distress and meet the needs of patients and families [25]. Traditional family meeting protocols require phenomenological modifications to first surface and bracket these pre-existing cultural frameworks before facilitating new understandings of filial duty within Chinese medical contexts. There are various tools available for standardized family meetings in the ICU [26], though no single optimal tool exists, and suitable tools can be selected based on specific needs.

In this study, nurses reported that patients’ strong desire to live often makes it difficult for them to accept any information about the imminent end of life. This intense will to live can lead to patients resisting end-of-life care [27]. Guiding patients and their families to accept the reality of life’s end is crucial. Research by Sinclair [28] indicates that empathy and compassion positively influence the patient care experience, making patients feel listened to, understood, and acknowledged. Dignity therapy and meaning-centered psychotherapy are two well-established, evidence-supported psychological interventions for end-of-life patients [26]. In the context of Chinese culture, ensuring these therapies are culturally sensitive is essential. Dignity Therapy can honor familial and collectivist values by guiding patients and their families to reflect on shared experiences, incorporating the Confucian concept of “legacy”. Meanwhile, Meaning-Centered Psychotherapy can integrate Buddhist and Taoist perspectives on life and death, helping patients approach mortality with greater acceptance and peace, thereby enhancing the localization and effectiveness of these interventions.

Communication failures further destabilize end-of-life care, where defensive practices—born of emotional exhaustion, institutional distrust, and chronic understaffing—reduce dialogue to mechanical updates, severing the intersubjective bonds essential for shared meaning-making. Husserl’s intentionality analysis exposes these ruptures as symptomatic of a deeper crisis: the displacement of care’s natural attitude by a technocratic intentionality that negates the lifeworld’s relational fabric. In China, this crisis is compounded by systemic gaps—end-of-life care education deficits, the absence of spiritual care roles (unlike Western chaplain-integrated models) due to cultural secularization [14], and workload pressures from nursing shortages amid rapid population aging [29]. Overburdened nurses, trained to prioritize acute clinical tasks over family communication, struggle to mediate cultural tensions like filial piety, exacerbating family-provider conflicts [30–32]. The resulting trust deficits among multidisciplinary teams mirror the broader collapse of lifeworld coherence, where protocol ambiguities and workflow fragmentation erode care continuity. Two reforms could restore relational intentionality: (1) Integrating WHO-based end-of-life care training into nursing curricula to bridge cultural and communication gaps; (2) Implementing interdisciplinary simulations and role-clarity guidelines to rebuild trust. Such interventions would realign systemic structures with the phenomenological imperative of care: sustaining shared meaning in the face of mortality.

ICU nurses in China face multidimensional emotional burdens—self-doubt, role conflicts, and ethical distress—when caring for terminally ill patients, exacerbated by systemic gaps in standardized end-of-life care education and fragmented clinical training. Unlike Western models with structured end-of-life care curricula and debriefing protocols [33, 34], China’s current training focuses narrowly on procedural skills, lacking the psychological and communicative scaffolding needed to navigate cultural-ethical tensions (e.g., filial piety’s demand for aggressive treatment versus end-of-life care principles). This dissonance reflects a theoretical duality: while nurses’ distress aligns with global patterns of compassion fatigue [9, 10, 35], its manifestation is uniquely shaped by China’s collectivist-care paradox—where institutional demands for efficiency clash with Confucian relational ethics. Empirical solutions like tiered training programs (pairing highly educated specialists with frontline staff) and mindfulness interventions [36] show cross-cultural promise, but their efficacy hinges on culturally attuned adaptations. For instance, bounded empathy—a pragmatic balance of engagement and detachment—must reconcile guanxi (relational reciprocity) with professional boundaries, avoiding Western individualism’s stark compartmentalization. The phenomenological distinctiveness lies in how nurses’ struggles expose a healthcare lifeworld bifurcated between technocratic urgency and unfulfilled relational duties. Reforms must address this duality: integrating WHO end-of-life care competencies into curricula to reframe care goals and institutionalizing reflective practices that honor both clinical pragmatism and cultural-ethical imperatives.

Conclusions

Implementing end-of-life care in the ICU presents a complex and multidimensional challenge, requiring comprehensive consideration of environmental optimization, cultural sensitivity education, team collaboration, and emotional support for nurses. Addressing the unique aspects of the ICU environment, such as the lack of privacy and the noisy atmosphere, is essential to creating a more tranquil and private space for patients and their families. Furthermore, given the cultural taboos surrounding death in traditional Chinese culture and the deep-rooted influence of Confucian filial piety, healthcare providers must guide families toward accepting end-of-life care with compassion and understanding. Promoting multidisciplinary team collaboration, clarifying role responsibilities, and establishing effective communication mechanisms are crucial for ensuring the professionalism and continuity of end-of-life care. Furthermore, recognizing the significant emotional burden borne by ICU nurses during caregiving, healthcare institutions should provide necessary psychological support and interventions to maintain the mental well-being of their staff.

Advantages and limitations

This study offers notable advantages, including qualitative interviews that captured ICU nurses’ firsthand experiences to illuminate end-of-life care practices, while extending beyond individual perspectives to examine organizational, collaborative, and familial dimensions of care, yielding culturally relevant insights for Chinese ICU settings. This study has several limitations: the small sample size may compromise generalizability, while unassessed variability in workplace end-of-life care training could impact transferability; regional disparities in culture, resources, and policies require consideration for practical application. We acknowledge that pre-existing rapport between researchers and participants established prior to data collection may introduce social desirability bias. Future research should specifically address the role of standardized end-of-life care education and training to enhance the quality and consistency of care delivery.

Implications for nursing practice

Our study reveals the multifaceted challenges and favorable conditions nurses encounter when delivering end-of-life care. In clinical practice, nurses continue to face systemic barriers such as insufficient organizational support, inefficiencies in team collaboration, unmet cultural needs of patients and families, and unaddressed emotional burdens. To enhance end-of-life care services, healthcare institutions must prioritize optimizing working conditions, advancing nurses’ specialized competencies through targeted training, and implementing structured psychological support systems.
