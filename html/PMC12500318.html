Abstract

Aberrant activation of AKT is a key oncogenic driver in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). As AKT activates multiple downstream signaling pathways, the key mechanisms mediating AKT-driven tumorigenesis must be elucidated to develop optimal treatment strategies. Using an Akt/NRas-induced HCC mouse model, we found that AKT promotes tumorigenesis by targeting TSC2 and GSK3Î±/Î² rather than FOXO family members. Loss of either TSC2, leading to mTORC1 activation, or both GSK3 isoforms cooperated with activated NRAS to promote HCC formation in vivo, albeit with different latencies. Simultaneous TSC2 and GSK3Î±/Î² deletion cooperated with NRAS to rapidly induce HCC formation, mirroring observations from the Akt/NRas HCC model. RNA-seq studies indicated distinct pathways regulated by TSC2/mTORC1 and GSK3Î±/Î² during hepatocarcinogenesis, with FOXM1 functioning as a major effector of GSK3. In summary, these findings uncover AKTâ€™s role in suppressing the TSC complex and GSK3 to drive HCC, offering mechanistic insights into oncogenic signaling and potential therapeutic targets.

Introduction

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is the most common type of primary liver cancer and the third leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide(1). The Food and Drug Administration has approved various therapies for advanced HCC(2), including tyrosine kinase inhibitors like sorafenib(3,4) and Lenvatinib(5), as well as a combination of immunotherapy and anti-angiogenesis therapy, namely atezolizumab and bevacizumab(5). However, improving overall survival and reducing recurrence in HCC patients remains challenging. Therefore, it is urgent to investigate novel and potentially effective therapeutic targets against this aggressive tumor.

The PI3K/AKT pathway plays a critical role in HCC development. Multiple genetic events leading to AKT activation have been reported in human HCC. For instance, activated mutations of PIK3CA regulate genes involved in various cellular processes such as proliferation, survival, migration, metabolism, differentiation, and glucose homeostasis(6â€“8). In this pathway, phosphorylation at T308 and S473 residues activates the AKT serine/threonine kinase. Once activated, AKT phosphorylates and inhibits some canonical downstream targets, such as forkhead box O protein (Foxo)(9,10), tuberous sclerosis complex 2 (TSC2)(11), and glycogen synthase kinase 3 (GSK3)(12). These AKT targets have also been implicated in HCC development and progression(7,13).

Foxo family members, especially Foxo1, have been considered the primary targets of AKT in the liver. Indeed, loss of Foxo1 has been able to rescue ablation of Akt1/2-induced insulin resistance, liver regeneration defects, and liver tumor development(14). However, the role(s) of Foxos, especially Foxo1, in mediating AKT's oncogenic potential are unknown.

Another critical AKT interactor is TSC2. Specifically, it inhibits HCC development by negatively regulating mTORC1(15), a pivotal downstream target of activated AKT signaling. However, while many studies have demonstrated that activated mTORC1 is necessary for AKT-driven cancer development in many tumor types(16,17), including HCC(18), limited data indicate that mTORC1 activation is sufficient for AKT's ability to induce liver tumor development.

GSK3 is a serine/threonine kinase consisting of two isoforms, GSK3Î± and GSK3Î², which AKT inhibits GSK3 via phosphorylation of GSK3Î± serine 21 and GSK3Î² serine 9 residues(6). Most studies suggest that GSK3 functions as a tumor suppressor in hepatocarcinogenesis, as lower GSK3Î² expression or higher levels of phosphorylated GSK3Î²Ser9 characterize HCC lesions compared to normal livers or surrounding non-tumorous tissues(19,20). The functional significance of GSK3Î±/Î² in mediating AKT's oncogenic role has not been explored to date.

Here, using in vitro and in vivo mouse models, we investigated the underlying molecular mechanism by which AKT executes its oncogenic program during hepatocarcinogenesis in the Akt/NRas-induced HCC mouse model (21) and human HCC.

Results

Role of Foxo family members in Akt/NRas-induced mouse hepatocarcinogenesis

Akt/NRas is an established mouse model to investigate the role of downstream targets of AKT during liver tumor development(21). Foxo proteins are canonical AKT substrates, and Foxo1 is considered the central Foxo family member in liver biology(32). Consistently, we observed increased phosphorylation/inactivation of Foxo1 at serine 256 (p-Foxo1Ser256) and downregulated Foxo downstream genes in liver tumors of Akt/NRas mice compared to normal livers (Fig. 1A; Supplementary Fig. S1A), implying that AKT suppresses Foxo1 activity during Akt/NRas-induced hepatocarcinogenesis. Thus, we hypothesized that Foxo1 is the major downstream effector during AKT-driven HCC formation. Therefore, loss of the Foxo1 gene combined with activated NRas should recapitulate hepatocarcinogenesis induced by Akt/NRas. To test this hypothesis, we injected two plasmids into Foxo1flox/flox mice by hydrodynamic tail vein injection. One plasmid carried the Cre recombinase driven by the cytomegalovirus promoter (pCMV-Cre) to knock out the Foxo1 gene, while the other construct contained the activated NRas gene (NrasV12). This strategy allowed the expression of NRas in Foxo1 KO hepatocytes (Foxo1âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas). The plasmids carrying the activated form of Akt and NRas genes were co-injected as a control (Akt/NRas) (Fig. 1B). Survival curve analysis showed that Akt/NRas mice exhibited a rapid onset of lethal tumor burden at 4â€“6 weeks after injection, in agreement with our previous study(21). However, Foxo1âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas mice did not show signs of tumor development and remained healthy even when harvested at ~22 weeks post-injection (Fig. 1C and D). Upon dissection, Akt/NRas mice exhibited numerous large tumor nodules occupying most of the liver parenchyma by 5 weeks post-injection. In contrast, no tumor nodules could be appreciated on most of Foxo1âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas mice; and only a few Foxo1âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas mice demonstrated small tumor nodules on the liver at 22 weeks post-injection (Fig. 1E; Supplementary Table 2). Immunohistochemical analysis showed few and small liver tumor lesions that were negative for Foxo1 expression and positive for p-ERK1/2 expression, consistent with the fact that these tumors were derived from Foxo1âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas hepatocytes. However, cell proliferation rate (as assessed by Ki67+ staining) was significantly lower than that from Akt/NRas liver tumors (Fig. 1E). These findings imply that Foxo1 knockout, associated with NRas activation, does not replicate Akt/NRas hepatocarcinogenesis. This suggests that Foxo1 inhibition is not the primary oncogenic signal during AKT-driven liver tumor formation.

As there are multiple Foxo family members, we repeated the experiments with a dominant negative form of Foxo (dnFoxo), which inhibits all Foxo members(33). Consistently, co-expression of dnFoxo and NRas did not trigger liver tumor formation in mice (Supplementary Fig. S1B and S1C).

Previous studies have shown that AKT-mediated phosphorylation of Foxo1 at T24, S256, and S319 residues causes Foxo1 translocation from the nucleus to the cytoplasm and attenuates its transcriptional activity(6,9). To investigate the role of AKT-mediated Foxo1 phosphorylation in HCC, we generated a plasmid carrying the mutant Foxo1 gene where all three phosphorylation sites (T24, S256, and S319) of Foxo1 were replaced with alanine (Foxo1AAA), rendering it unable to be phosphorylated by AKT and a constitutively activated Foxo1. Next, the Foxo1AAA construct (pT3-Foxo1AAA) was co-injected with Akt and NRas plasmids into the wild-type mice (Akt/NRas/Foxo1AAA) (Fig. 1F). Additional mice were injected with Akt, NRas, and pT3-EF1Î± empty vector as the control (Akt/NRas/pT3). Notably, overexpression of Foxo1AAA did not inhibit Akt/NRas-driven hepatocarcinogenesis. All mice developed large tumors and were required to be euthanized by 7 to 8 weeks post-injection (Fig. 1G). No significant differences in tumor size (Fig. 1H) and liver weight (Supplementary Fig. S1D) between the Akt/NRas/Foxo1AAA and control Akt/NRas/pT3 groups were detected at this time point. Histological evaluation revealed that overexpression of Foxo1AAA did not affect the liver tumor phenotype of Akt/NRas mice (Fig. 1H), and tumor cells from both cohorts of mice were highly proliferative. These results demonstrate that constitutively activated Foxo1 is unable to inhibit Akt/NRas-induced HCC.

In summary, the findings indicate that Foxo transcription factors are not the key downstream targets of AKT during Akt/NRas-induced hepatocarcinogenesis.

TSC/mTORC1 pathway during Akt/NRas-induced hepatocarcinogenesis

AKT inhibits TSC2 activity via direct phosphorylation, implying TSC2 as an AKT substrate(6,11). TSC/mTORC1 has been considered the major signaling in AKT-driven tumor formation. This notion was substantiated by the fact that loss of mTORC1 by Raptor ablation strongly inhibits Akt/NRas-dependent hepatocarcinogenesis(34). However, the question remains whether the activation of mTORC1 alone is sufficient to replicate activated AKT and synergizes with NRas to promote liver tumor development. To address this question, we employed the conditional Tsc2 KO mice (Tsc2flox/flox mice). Thus, we injected the pCMV-Cre plasmid to delete the Tsc2 gene, together with the NRas plasmid, into Tsc2flox/flox mice (Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas). Additional Tsc2flox/flox mice were injected with Akt/NRas plasmid as the control (Akt/NRas) (Fig. 2A). Survival curve analysis revealed that lethal tumor burden was observed in Akt/NRas mice within 4~6 weeks after injection (Fig. 2B), similar to our previous study(21). In contrast, large tumor burden occurred in Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas mice only after 18 weeks post-injection (Fig. 2B and C). Consistent with this observation, liver weight in Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas mice prior to 20 weeks post-injection was lower than in Akt/NRas (Tsc2+/+ genetic background) mice (Fig. 2D). At the late stage (more than 20 weeks post-injection) of tumor development, liver weight in Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas mice increased to levels comparable to those in Akt/NRas mice (Fig. 2D).

Histological evaluation revealed that Akt/NRas liver lesions consisted of highly malignant HCC cells, with some regions exhibiting cholangiocarcinoma (CCA)-like features (as assessed by Ck19+ staining). In contrast, Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas tumors consisted almost exclusively of well or moderately differentiated HCC lesions, and only rarely CCA-like tumors developed (Fig. 2C and E). Tumor cell proliferation rate was also lower in Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas than in Akt/NRas lesions (Fig. 2C and F). Previous studies have shown that AKT mediates the inhibition of TSC activity, leading to mTORC1 pathway activation (6,35). Thus, we analyzed AKT and mTORC1 activation status in Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas and Tsc2+/+/Akt/NRas HCC lesions using Western blotting (Fig. 2G). As expected, mTORC1 was activated, which could be viewed as high expression of p-p70S6K, p-RPS6, and p-4EBP1, in both Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas and Akt/NRas tumors, whereas a strong AKT activation occurred only in Akt/NRas HCC (Fig. 2G).

Overall, we discovered that the loss of Tsc2, which leads to the activation of mTORC1, works in conjunction with NRas to induce HCC formation in vivo. However, HCC development occurs over a significantly longer period, and the tumors were less aggressive compared to those found in Akt/NRas mice. These findings suggest that the activation of mTORC1 alone is not sufficient to fully replicate the oncogenic potential of activated AKT during the formation of HCC; additional signals are necessary for this process.

GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² are the critical AKT downstream targets in hepatocarcinogenesis.

The relatively restricted impact of Foxo family members and TSC2/mTORC1 pathway in mediating AKT oncogenic activities indicated that additional pathways might be the major signaling event downstream of activated AKT. Since AKT inhibits the activity of GSK3, which has two isoforms â€”GSK3Î± and GSK3Î²â€” phosphorylated at serine 21 and serine 9 residues by AKT, respectively, we hypothesized that deleting either GSK3Î± and/or GSK3Î² could recapitulate the development of HCC observed in the Akt/NRas mice. In support of our hypothesis, we observed increased levels of p-GSK3Î±Ser21 and p-GSK3Î²Ser9 in the tumor lesions of Akt/NRas mice (Fig. 3A). Next, we identified single guide RNA (sgRNA) sequences against mouse GSK3Î± or GSK3Î² gene to effectively inhibit GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² expression in vitro (Supplementary Fig. S2A and S2B). As GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² might have redundant roles, we simultaneously knocked out GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² genes using a single CRISPR plasmid carrying sgRNA sequences targeting both GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² (sgGSK3Î±/Î²) (Supplementary Fig. S2C) and co-injected it with NRas plasmid into FVB/N mice (sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas). Either of CRISPR plasmid carrying sgRNA sequence against GFP gene or Akt plasmid together with NRas plasmid were co-injected as the control (sgGFP/NRas and Akt/NRas, respectively) (Fig. 3B). Consistent with our previous studies, Akt/NRas co-expression caused lethal tumor burden within 6 weeks post-injection, whereas activation of NRas alone (as seen in sgGFP/NRas mice) did not induce liver tumor formation (Fig. 3C). Interestingly, the sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas mice became moribund due to a lethal tumor burden by approximately 10 weeks post-injection, which was only moderately slower than those injected with Akt and NRas oncogenes (Fig. 3C). Western blotting and IHC confirmed that sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas liver tumors expressed GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² at very low level (Fig. 3D and E). Histologically, sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas livers were occupied by HCC and CCA-like lesions, similar to those developing in Akt/NRas livers (Fig. 3E; Supplementary Fig. S3). The tumor proliferation rate and the number of CCA-like tumors were lower in sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas mice compared to Akt/NRas livers (Fig. 3Eâ€“G).

Next, we investigated whether deleting GSK3Î± or GSK3Î² alone can synergize with activated NRas to promote HCC formation. Therefore, we co-injected either sgGFP, sgGSK3Î±, or sgGSK3Î² with NRas plasmids into FVB/N mice (Supplementary Fig. S4A) and harvested the mice at 14 weeks post-injection. Several small tumor nodules developed in sgGSK3Î±/NRas and sgGSK3Î²/NRas mice, whereas no tumor lesions occurred in sgGFP/NRas mice (Supplementary Fig. S4B; Supplementary Table S2), in striking contrast to the numerous tumor lesions developing in sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas livers. The tumor cells were proliferative, as demonstrated by Ki67 immunoreactivity (Supplementary Fig. S5A and S5B). sgGSK3Î±/NRas and sgGSK3Î²/NRas lesions were predominantly HCC-like tumors with only a few CCA-like lesions (Supplementary Fig. S5A and S5C). The results suggest that GSK3Î± alone or GSK3Î² alone have weak tumor-suppressing roles, and the two isoforms likely function redundantly to inhibit hepatocarcinogenesis.

In summary, the combined loss of GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² works synergistically with activated NRas to promote liver tumor formation within a relatively short period. This finding supports the hypothesis that the GSK3 pathway may be a key signaling mechanism mediating the effects of AKT during hepatocarcinogenesis. The study also highlights the significant tumor suppressor role of GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² in vivo. Furthermore, the sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas model represents the first murine model of tumors induced by the simultaneous loss of GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² genes.

AKT-mediated phosphorylation of GSK3Î±S21 and GSK3Î²S9 is the critical event in Akt/NRas-induced hepatocarcinogenesis

To complement the studies mentioned above, we determined whether constitutively activated GSK3Î± or GSK3Î² can inhibit Akt/NRas-driven liver tumor formation in vivo, based on the observation that the immunoreactivity of p-GSK3Î±Ser21 and p-GSK3Î²Ser9 was higher in Akt/NRas-induced liver tumors than in surrounding non-tumorous tissue (Fig. 3A). Thus, we introduced mutations in Myc-tagged plasmids for GSK3Î±Ser21 and GSK3Î²Ser9 to substitute with alanine (GSK3Î±S21A-Myc-tag and GSK3Î²S9A-Myc-tag, respectively), which prevented their phosphorylation by AKT. Next, we co-injected the plasmids carrying either GSK3Î±S21A, GSK3Î²S9A, or a pT3 empty vector, with activated Akt and NRas plasmids into FVB/N mice (Fig. 4A). Overexpression of GSK3Î±S21A or GSK3Î²S9A significantly delayed Akt/NRas-induced tumor development, as evidenced by the longer lifespan of mice injected with GSK3Î±S21A or GSK3Î²S9A plasmid compared to the control mice (Fig. 4B). Remarkably, smaller tumor lesions developed in GSK3Î±S21A and GSK3Î²S9A mice compared to the large tumor nodules observed in Akt/NRas/pT3 mice at 5.4 weeks post-injection. The tumor nodules in mice injected with GSK3Î±S21A or GSK3Î²S9A only reached a late stage of development at 15 or 18 weeks post-injection, as assessed by H&E staining (Fig. 4C). Positive immunoreactivity for p-AKTSer473 and p-ERK1/2 was detected in all tumor lesions, suggesting that AKT and NRas were still active in the tumor cells (Supplementary Fig. S6). Additionally, overexpression of GSK3Î±S21A or GSK3Î²S9A reduced liver weight, cell proliferative ability (Ki67+), and CCA lesions (CK19+), at the same time point (Fig. 4Dâ€“F).

Altogether, the results indicate that overexpression of GSK3Î±S21A or GSK3Î²S9A significantly delays Akt/NRas-driven tumor development, and that AKT-mediated phosphorylation of GSK3Î±S21 and GSK3Î²S9 is crucial for Akt/NRas-induced hepatocarcinogenesis. Therefore, the suppression of GSK3Î± and GSK3Î² activities is a critical signal mediating the AKT oncogenic potential in liver tumorigenesis.

Distinct pathways are mediated by TSC2/mTORC1 and GSK3Î±/Î² in Akt/NRas-driven hepatocarcinogenesis

To further illustrate the molecular mechanisms underlying TSC2/mTORC1 and GSK3Î±/Î² mediating AKT's oncogenic potential during liver tumor development, we analyzed global gene expression patterns of liver tumors collected from Akt/NRas, Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas, and sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas mice, with normal liver (NL) from the uninjected mice as the control (Fig. 5A). Principal component analysis (PCA) revealed distinct gene expression patterns of the tumors from normal liver tissues (Fig. 5A). Importantly, different expression profiles characterized Akt/NRas, Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas, and sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas liver tumors. Specifically, we identified 1700 genes up-regulated in tumor samples and 1126 genes downregulated in all tumors (Fig. 5B; Supplementary Data S1). These genes are related to pathways involved in cancer, PI3K/AKT signaling pathway, and activated Ras/MAPK signaling pathway (Supplementary Fig. S7).

To elucidate the unique gene expression patterns regulated by TSC2/mTORC1 or GSK3Î±/Î² signaling, we identified genes that were upregulated in Akt/NRas liver tumors and only upregulated in Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas liver tumors, but not in sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas liver tumors as TSC2-upregulated genes (n=262) (Fig. 5B; Supplementary Data S2). We applied a similar strategy to identify TSC2-downregulated genes (n=165) (Fig. 5B; Supplementary Data S3), GSK3Î±/Î²-upregulated genes (n=827) (Fig. 5B; Supplementary Data S4), and GSK3Î±/Î²-downregulated genes (n=748) (Fig. 5B; Supplementary Data S5). Pathway analysis revealed that the TSC2/mTORC1 pathway uniquely upregulates genes involved in the actin cytoskeleton, Rap1 signaling, focal adhesion, and apoptosis in Akt/NRas-induced liver tumors (Fig. 5C; Supplementary Fig. S8A). In contrast, GSK3Î±/Î² upregulated genes were primarily involved in cell cycle and DNA replication in Akt/NRas-induced liver tumors (Fig. 5D; Supplementary Fig. S8B), identifying GSK3Î±/Î² as a critical AKT downstream effector promoting tumor cell proliferation. Concerning downregulated genes, TSC2/mTORC1 and GSK3Î±/Î² regulate different metabolic pathways downstream of activated AKT in HCC (Supplementary Fig. S9).

In summary, TSC2/mTORC1 and GSK3Î±/Î² regulate distinct pathways downstream of AKT during hepatocarcinogenesis.

The AKT/GSK3 axis regulates FOXM1 in HCC

Numerous studies implied mTORC1 as the major downstream effector of AKT in tumors, including HCC(18,34,36). However, the functional role of GSK3Î±/Î² as the downstream effectors of AKT during liver tumor development remains poorly characterized. Therefore, we focused on genes uniquely regulated by GSK3Î±/Î² in the RNASeq approach and selected genes that are upregulated in human HCC samples and have prognostic values. Among these genes is forkhead box M1 (Foxm1), which has been previously linked to HCC(21,37). Foxm1 expression is upregulated in human HCC samples, and high expression of Foxm1 is associated with poor patient survival (Fig. 6A and B). Our RNAseq study revealed that Foxm1 expression is strongly upregulated in Akt/NRas and sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas tumor lesions, but not in Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas tumors (Fig. 6C).

As the first step to investigate the functions of FOXM1 in hepatocarcinogenesis, we analyzed genes whose expression patterns are correlated with that of Foxm1 in human HCC samples from the TCGA database (Supplementary Data S6). Pathway analysis revealed that Foxm1 positively correlates with genes associated with cell cycle and DNA replication, and it negatively correlates with genes associated with metabolic pathways, etc. (Supplementary Fig. S10). The data are consistent with the hypothesis that FOXM1 might be a key molecule downstream of AKT/GSK3 cascade in hepatocarcinogenesis (Fig. 5D). Next, we analyzed how FOXM1 modulates HCC cell growth in vitro. We retrieved the FOXM1 perturbation effect data from the DepMap portal. Notably, CRISPR-based deletion of FOXM1 induced growth restraint in most of the HCC cell lines tested (Supplementary Fig. S11), supporting the requirement of FOXM1 for HCC cell growth in vitro.

Finally, we investigate the importance of FOXM1 in Akt/NRas- and sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas-dependent hepatocarcinogenesis. Therefore, we designed a single guide RNA against mouse Foxm1 and cloned it into a pX330 vector (Supplementary Fig. S12A). The knock-out efficiency of sgFoxm1 was evaluated in the mouse HCC cell line HCC3â€“4 using Western blot analysis (Supplementary Fig. S12B). Subsequently, we hydrodynamically injected Akt/NRas plasmids with sgFoxm1 into the mice (Fig. 6D). TIDE analysis showed the effectiveness of sgFoxm1 in disrupting the Foxm1 allele in the tumor cells (Supplementary Fig. S12C). In agreement with the in vitro evidence, the deletion of Foxm1 significantly delayed Akt/NRas-driven liver tumor formation (Fig. 6E and F). However, the eventual tumors remained proliferative as demonstrated by Ki67 IHC (Supplementary Fig. S12D). The results suggested that Foxm1 deletion suppressed but could not completely inhibit Akt/NRas-induced liver tumor development. Similar results were obtained in sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas-injected mice (Fig. 6Gâ€“I; Supplementary Fig. S12D).

Overall, our studies suggest that FOXM1 is a major factor regulated by AKT/GSK3 in HCC.

Concomitant deletion of GSK3Î±/Î² and TSC2 together with NRas activation rapidly induces liver tumor formation in mice

Given the significant tumor suppressive roles of GSK3Î±/Î² and TSC2 proteins in hepatocarcinogenesis, we hypothesized that the concurrent deletion of the GSK3Î±, GSK3Î², and Tsc2 genes, along with the expression of active NRas, could drive hepatocarcinogenesis similar to that observed in Akt/NRas mice. To investigate this hypothesis, we co-injected pCMV-Cre and NRas plasmids into Tsc2flox/flox mice with or without the sgGSK3Î±/Î² plasmid (Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î²) (Fig. 7A). Akt/NRas or sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas plasmids were injected into FVB/N mice as a control (Fig. 7A). Remarkably, concomitant deletion of GSK3Î±/Î² and Tsc2 induced tumor development with the same latency as observed in Akt/NRas mice (Fig. 7B and C). Furthermore, cell proliferative activity (Ki67+) and CCA-like lesions (CK19+) were evident in liver tumor tissue from Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î² mice (Fig. 7D). At the histopathological level, significant differences were observed between the liver lesions of Akt/NRas mice and Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î² mice (Supplementary Fig. S13). Specifically, the livers of Akt/NRas mice contained large lipid-rich preneoplastic cells, consistent with previous findings(21). The hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) cells in Akt/NRas mice were small, highly basophilic, and contained tiny lipid droplets. Additionally, cholangiocellular and mixed hepatocellular/cholangiocellular lesions were consistently present in the Akt/NRas liver parenchyma (Supplementary Fig. S13A and S13B). In contrast, Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î² livers showed no visible preneoplastic cells, indicating that lesions in this model presumably do not progress through a premalignant phase. Liver tumors were histologically less aggressive than those observed in Akt/NRas mice. The HCC cells in these livers were extremely large in size and displayed a large and vacuolated eosinophilic cytoplasm. While cholangiocellular lesions were present in Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î² livers, they were significantly less numerous compared to those in Akt/NRas mice (Supplementary Fig. S13C and S13D).

Subsequently, we analyzed the gene expression patterns of Akt/NRas liver tumors versus Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î² mouse liver tumors using RNASeq (Supplementary Fig. S14A). We identified 2342 commonly upregulated genes compared to the normal liver in both Akt/NRas and Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î² liver tumors, and these genes were involved in cancer pathways, PI3K/AKT signaling, MAPK, cell cycle, etc. (Supplementary Fig. S14B and S14C). We also identified 1695 genes that were commonly downregulated compared to normal liver, involved in metabolic pathways (Supplementary Fig. S14B and S14D). Intriguingly, we discovered significant differences in the overall gene expression patterns between the two liver tumor specimens (Supplementary Fig. S14A and S14B). For instance, transcriptional misregulation in cancer and Notch signaling were uniquely upregulated in Akt/NRas liver tumor tissues (Supplementary Fig. S15).

These results indicate that simultaneous deletion of GSK3Î±/Î² and Tsc2 and activation of NRas rapidly induce liver tumor formation, similar to that observed in Akt/NRas mice. Nevertheless, histopathologic and gene expression analyses strongly suggest that additional genes or pathways might function as modifiers, leading to the additional changes responsible for the diverse morphologic and molecular phenotypes observed in Akt/NRas and Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î² mice.

Activated AKT is associated with the inactivation of GSK3Î±/Î² in human HCC samples

Our study demonstrates that inactivation of GSK3Î±/Î² and activation of mTORC1 may be key signaling events downstream of activated AKT during hepatocarcinogenesis. Therefore, we first analyzed the activation/inactivation status of AKT (p-AKT) and GSK3Î±/Î² (p-GSK3Î± and p-GSK3Î²) in our collection of human HCC samples (n=108). Immunoreactivity for activated/phosphorylated AKT (p-AKT) could be found in 43/108 (40%) of HCC samples analyzed, whereas inactivated/phosphorylated GSK3Î± and/or 3Î² could be seen in 50/108 (46%) of cases (Fig. 8A and B). Importantly, 40/43 (93%) p-AKT (+) human HCCs demonstrated either p-GSK3Î± and/or -3Î² immunoreactivity (Fig. 8Aâ€“C). In contrast, 55/65 (85%) of p-AKT (â€“) HCCs were negative for both GSK3Î± and 3Î² (Fig. 8A and B). The difference was found to be statistically significant (Fig. 8D). Furthermore, analysis of The Cancer Proteome Atlas (TCPA) data revealed a strong positive correlation between p-AKTSer473/p-GSK3Ser9 and FOXM1 expression in human HCC samples (n = 184) (Fig. 8E; Supplementary Fig. S16), supporting the hypothesis that the GSK3Î±/Î²â€“FOXM1 cascade is a key signaling axis downstream of activated AKT during hepatocarcinogenesis.

We next examined mTORC1 activation in human HCC samples. In line with findings from the Akt/NRas HCC mouse model, TCPA data showed a strong positive correlation between p-AKTSer473 and mTORC1 activation markers, including p-mTORSer2448 and p-P70S6KThr389 (Supplementary Fig. S17), indicating that mTORC1 activation is closely associated with AKT signaling in human HCC. Additionally, mTORC1 activation positively correlated with GSK3 inactivation in the same cohort, suggesting that mTORC1 activation and GSK3Î±/Î² inactivation co-occur in AKT-driven human HCC (Supplementary Fig. S18). Together with the findings from mouse models, these human data further support that GSK3/FOXM1 and TSC2/mTORC1 pathways are major downstream effectors of AKT activation in hepatocarcinogenesis.

Discussion

The PI3K/AKT pathway plays a crucial role in the development and progression of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). However, the specific mechanisms through which AKT promotes its oncogenic activity in liver cancer formation are still not fully understood. In this study, we systematically examined the functional significance of three key downstream effectors of oncogenic AKT signaling: Foxo, TSC/mTORC1, and GSK3Î±/Î², in the context of hepatocarcinogenesis.

The first conclusion from this study is that the Foxo pathway has a limited role in the formation of AKT-driven hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). This finding contrasts with previous studies, which indicated that the loss of Foxo1 could rescue defects in insulin signaling and liver regeneration resulting from the ablation of Akt1/2(14,38). Here, we found that the loss of Foxo1 did not significantly cooperate with activated Ras to induce HCC formation. Importantly, overexpression of the constitutively activated form of Foxo1 (Foxo1AAA) did not inhibit liver tumor formation induced by Akt/NRas. These data suggest that the loss of Foxo1 might occur later during HCC progression, at least in tumors co-expressing activated NRas and AKT. However, our findings do not exclude the possibility that the loss of Foxo1 function may still regulate HCC cells at the molecular level. To investigate this hypothesis, we performed RNA sequencing analysis comparing Akt/NRas tumors with Akt/NRas/Foxo1AAA tumors. We identified 506 genes that are differentially expressed between the two tumor types (Supplementary Fig. S19; Supplementary Data S7). The results indicate that Foxo1 may influence other tumor properties, such as differentiation status and drug resistance. Further analysis will be necessary to elucidate the roles of Foxo1 protein during hepatocarcinogenesis.

The mTOR pathway has been considered the major signaling event downstream of activated AKT. We have previously shown that loss of mTORC1 via deleting its unique subunit Raptor completely suppresses Akt/NRas-driven HCC formation(34), revealing that mTORC1 is required for tumor development. In the current study, using conditional Tsc2 KO mice, we demonstrated that activation of mTORC1 alone cooperates with activated RAS to promote HCC formation, albeit after longer latency, and the tumors appeared to be much less aggressive. RNAseq analysis revealed the distinct gene expression patterns between Akt/NRas and Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas liver tumor specimens. These data indicate that additional signaling pathways are required downstream of AKT in addition to the activated mTORC1 to promote HCC progression and aggressiveness.

The third pathway that we analyzed is GSK3Î±/Î² signaling in AKT-driven liver tumor development. Using human HCC samples, we demonstrated that activated AKT is frequently associated with the increased phosphorylation/inactivation of GSK3Î± and/or GSK3Î² (Fig. 8). Notably, we show here that the ablation of GSK3Î±/Î² synergizes with NRas to promote HCC formation, albeit after slightly longer latency then that detected in Akt/NRas mice. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first murine model with loss of GSK3Î±/Î² and represents a new tool to study this tumor suppressor pathway in tumor development. RNAseq analysis revealed that GSK3Î±/Î² controlled major signaling events downstream of activated AKT in HCC, including cell proliferation. Importantly, we identified FOXM1 as a target downregulated by AKT/GSK3Î±/Î² in HCC. CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockout of Foxm1 significantly delayed HCC formation in Akt/NRas and sgGSK3Î±/Î²/NRas mouse models (Fig. 6). These data unravel the key role of the GSK3Î±/Î²/FOXM1 axis during hepatocarcinogenesis. The precise mechanisms by which FOXM1 is regulated by AKT/GSK3 cascade require further investigation. As GSK3Î±/Î² are kinases, it is likely that their regulation of Foxm1 at mRNA levels is indirect. One possible link is that GSK3Î±/Î² are known to regulate the stability of YAP (39). Multiple studies have shown that FOXM1 is a direct target of YAP (40,41). It would be important to investigate whether FOXM1 is regulated by AKT/GSK3 cascade via YAP during HCC pathogenesis.

Finally, we showed that simultaneous deletion of GSK3Î±&3Î² and activated mTORC1 synergize with activated NRas to rapidly induce liver tumor formation in mice, recapitulating Akt/NRas-induced hepatocarcinogenesis. These results provide strong evidence that GSK3Î±/Î² and TSC/mTORC1 cascades are the major cellular events that act downstream of activated AKT to promote liver tumorigenesis. Intriguingly, RNAseq studies revealed that at the molecular level, distinct gene expression patterns between Akt/NRas and Tsc2âˆ’/âˆ’/NRas/sgGSK3Î±/Î² mouse liver tumors were unveiled. The results indicate that other pathways may play significant roles in modulating tumor expression patterns without directly influencing tumor formation. As we discussed earlier, one of these signaling pathways involves the Foxo family of members. In fact, further RNA sequencing studies showed that Foxo1AAA was capable of regulating several pathways in Akt/NRas tumors.

The study demonstrates that the GSK3Î±/Î² and TSC/mTORC1 pathways play crucial roles as downstream effectors in AKT-driven hepatocarcinogenesis. Additionally, Foxo proteins may act as molecular modifiers in this process. Our findings carry significant translational implications, suggesting that targeting both the TSC/mTORC1 and GSK3Î±/Î² pathways may be important for treating human HCC subsets with activated AKT.
