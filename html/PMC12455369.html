Key Points

Question

Is ammoxetine safe and effective for treating major depressive disorder (MDD)?

Findings

In this randomized clinical trial in 239 adult patients with MDD, both ammoxetine 40 mg/d and 60 mg/d significantly decreased the total score of the Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale from baseline to week 8 compared with placebo. The incidence and nature of adverse events were similar among groups.

Meaning

These findings support the safety and efficacy of ammoxetine in the treatment of MDD, providing a basis for phase 3 validation.

This randomized clinical trial evaluates the efficacy and safety of ammoxetine 60 mg/d and 40 mg/d vs placebo in treating adult major depressive disorder.

Abstract

Importance

Major depressive disorder (MDD) is among the most prevalent mental health disorders, causing substantial disability and economic burden. Although several first-line treatments exist with mild adverse effects, up to 50% to 60% of patients do not tolerate or respond to them. Ammoxetine, a novel selective serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor, has been found to reduce adverse effects and hepatotoxicity and more potent inhibition of serotonin and norepinephrine transporters, making it more tolerable and effective.

Objective

To evaluate the efficacy and safety of ammoxetine in treating adults with MDD.

Design, Setting, and Participants

This phase 2 randomized clinical trial was a multicenter, double-masked, placebo-controlled, parallel-group, fixed-dose study of ammoxetine treatment in patients with MDD in China. Patients aged 18 to 65 years from 15 study centers were randomized into daily ammoxetine or placebo groups between March 27, 2023, and June 13, 2024, and followed up for 10 weeks.

Interventions

Participants were randomized 1:1:1 to 3 treatment groups: ammoxetine 40 mg/d, ammoxetine 60 mg/d, and placebo.

Main Outcome and Measures

The primary outcome was change in Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale (MADRS) total score from baseline to 8 weeks. Efficacy analyses were performed on both the full-analysis and per-protocol sets using least-squares (LS) mean differences. The safety set analysis was performed using descriptive statistics.

Results

Among 239 enrolled patients (mean [SD] age, 30.4 [10.0] years, 158 female [66.1%]), 80 were randomized to the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, 80 to the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group, and 79 to the placebo group. In the full-analysis set, both ammoxetine doses led to statistically significant improvements in MADRS total scores at week 8 compared with placebo. The LS mean changes (SE) from baseline were −16.7 (1.3) for ammoxetine 40 mg/d, −16.6 (1.3) for ammoxetine 60 mg/d, and −13.5 (1.3) for placebo. The differences vs placebo were −3.3 (97.3 CI, −6.3 to −0.3) for ammoxetine 40 mg/d and −3.1 (97.3% CI, −6.2 to 0.0) for ammoxetine 60 mg/d. Consistent results were observed in the per-protocol set analysis at week 8 for ammoxetine 40 mg/d (LS mean change, −3.2; 97.3% CI, −6.2 to −0.2) and ammoxetine 60 mg/d (LS mean change, −3.18; 97.3% CI, −6.2 to −0.2), both superior to placebo. Treatment-emergent adverse events were reported in 68 participants (85.0%) receiving ammoxetine 60 mg/d, 63 (78.8%) receiving ammoxetine 40 mg/d, and 48 (60.8%) receiving placebo; most were mild to moderate in severity.

Conclusions and Relevance

This randomized clinical trial demonstrated superiority over placebo of ammoxetine treatment at both 40 mg/d and 60 mg/d in patients with MDD. In addition, all doses of ammoxetine were generally well tolerated.

Introduction

Major depressive disorder (MDD) is among the most prevalent mental health disorders, causing substantial disability and economic burden.1,2 The World Health Organization reported that MDD will become the primary cause of disability worldwide by 2030.3 Various classes of antidepressant medications include selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), selective serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs), mirtazapine, tricyclic antidepressants, and monoamine oxidase inhibitors, among others.4 Due to their relatively mild adverse effects, SSRIs and SNRIs are often recommended as first-line treatment for MDD.5 However, 50% to 60% of patients cannot tolerate or do not respond adequately to them.6 The STAR*D study indicated that only 30% to 40% of patients reached remission with first-line antidepressants.7 Additionally, these medications have a slow onset of action and a high incidence of adverse reactions.8,9 The SNRIs may outperform SSRIs in efficacy and speed of onset, with fewer cardiovascular complications compared with tricyclic antidepressants and monoamine oxidase inhibitors.10,11,12 Furthermore, SNRIs effectively alleviate depressive symptoms associated with chronic pain, making them mainstream options in clinical practice.13 Compared with SSRIs, the variety of SNRIs is limited, with duloxetine dominating the market.14 While it blocks serotonin and norepinephrine transporters more potently than venlafaxine,15 many patients with MDD still experience common adverse effects, such as nausea, drowsiness, dry mouth, sweating, constipation, and decreased appetite. Moreover, duloxetine may have higher hepatotoxicity than other SNRIs, such as venlafaxine,16,17 because of its naphthyl ring, which induces oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction in hepatic organoids.18 Therefore, considering the burden of MDD and the potential of SNRIs, there is a need for new compounds within this class.

Ammoxetine, a novel SNRI developed from duloxetine,19 addresses hepatotoxicity through structural modification by replacing the naphthyl ring with benzodioxole, which significantly reduces hepatotoxic effects.20 Additionally, in vitro, ammoxetine exhibits a threefold to 10-fold greater potency than duloxetine in inhibiting serotonin and norepinephrine transporters.21 In vivo, ammoxetine has shown stronger antidepressant effects in animal models with lower neuronal and hepatocyte toxicity.21,22,23 Two phase 1 trials24,25 in healthy volunteers demonstrated that all adverse events were mild and self-resolving, including nausea, dizziness, palpitations, and dry mouth, with no liver-related adverse event noted.26 Furthermore, a high-throughput assay showed that ammoxetine weakly inhibits CYP2D6 and CYP1A2, with minimal effects on other CYP enzymes, indicating low drug interaction risk.20 Based on these findings, ammoxetine has been approved for this clinical trial to evaluate its efficacy and safety in patients with MDD.

Methods

Study Design

This randomized clinical trial was a phase 2, multicenter (15 sites in China), double-masked, placebo-controlled, parallel-group study. The study was conducted in accordance with International Council for Harmonization Good Clinical Practice principles and the Declaration of Helsinki.27 All participants provided written informed consent prior to enrollment, and ethical approval was obtained from local research ethics committees. The trial protocol is provided in Supplement 1. This study was reported in accordance with the Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials (CONSORT).

The trial included a screening period of 6 to 14 days (with a single-masked placebo run-in period of 5-7 days), an 8-week double-masked treatment period (1 week for titration and 7 weeks for dose maintenance), a 1-week double-masked dose reduction, and a 1-week safety follow-up. After signing the informed consent form, participants received placebo for 5 to 7 consecutive days. Participants were assigned in a 1:1:1 ratio using stratified blocked randomization to either the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group, the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, or the placebo group via an interactive web response system. Stratified factors included baseline severity (Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale [MADRS] score >30 or 26-30, indicating moderate to severe depression) and patient diagnostic type (single vs recurrent episode).

Participants

Participants aged 18 to 65 years with MDD based on Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (Fifth Edition) criteria28 were recruited between March 27, 2023, and June 13, 2024. Inclusion criteria included a total score of 26 or higher on the MADRS, a Clinical Global Impressions–Severity (CGI-S) score of 4 or higher, and a Hamilton Rating Scale for Depression (HAMD) score of 2 or higher on the first item at screening visits. Key exclusion criteria included a reduction rate of MADRS score of 25% or greater at baseline compared with the screening period; meeting Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (Fifth Edition) criteria for other psychiatric disorders; depressive disorder secondary to other mental illnesses or physical conditions; and receipt of electroconvulsive therapy, systematic psychotherapy, transcranial magnetic stimulation, vagus nerve stimulation, or phototherapy within 3 months prior to screening. The full inclusion and exclusion criteria are listed in Supplement 1.

Procedures and Outcomes

The study medication included ammoxetine tablets (40 mg and 60 mg) and placebo tablets, all identical in appearance. Participants took the medication every morning at a fixed time (1 hour plus or minus 30 minutes) after meals and recorded both medication and meal times. The specific medication plan is detailed in eTable 1 in Supplement 2. During the treatment period, outcome indicators were collected at each visit.

The primary efficacy end point was the change in MADRS total score from baseline to week 8. Secondary efficacy end points included changes from baseline in scores measured at the end of week 8 or at each visit, including MADRS; 17-Item HAMD (HAMD-17) total score; CGI-S score; CGI-Improvement (CGI-I) score; Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale (HAMA) total score; response rates in MADRS and HAMD-17 (≥50% decrease); remission rates in MADRS (≤10) and HAMD-17 (≤7); and the percentage of participants achieving an MADRS score reduction of 25% or more at weeks 1 and 2. Safety assessments included adverse events, withdrawal due to adverse events, vital signs, weight, physical examination, laboratory tests, 12-lead electrocardiography, the Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale, the Arizona Sexual Experience Scale, and the proportion of participants receiving combined hypnotic drugs.

Statistical Analysis

Efficacy analyses were performed on both the full-analysis set and the per-protocol set. Safety data analysis was based on the safety set. The final analysis compared the 2 treatment groups with the placebo group. Dunnett method was used to correct for type I errors, with the primary end point tested at a 1-sided α = .01; success was achieved when at least 1 experimental group outperformed the placebo.

The primary end point was analyzed using analysis of covariance. The model included baseline MADRS score as a covariate, with treatment group (ammoxetine 40 mg/d, ammoxetine 60 mg/d, or placebo) and diagnosis type (a single episode of MDD or recurrent MDD) as fixed effects. Least-squares (LS) mean differences (compared with placebo), SEs, and corresponding 95% CIs or 97.3% CIs were estimated for the change from baseline at week 8.

Detailed methodologies for missing data imputation, sensitivity analyses, subgroup analyses, secondary end point analyses, and sample size calculations are provided in the eMethods in Supplement 2. Descriptive statistics were used to summarize demographic and safety data. All analyses were performed using SAS, version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc).

Results

A total of 290 patients were screened, and 239 (mean [SD] age, 30.4 [10.0] years; 158 female [66.1%] and 81 male [33.9%]) were randomized and received therapy, with 80 in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, 80 in the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group, and 79 in the placebo group (Figure 1). These patients were included in both the full-analysis set for efficacy evaluation and the safety set for safety analysis. Demographics, clinical characteristics, and baseline MADRS scores were similar among groups (Table 1). Eighteen participants (7.5%) with missing week 8 MADRS scores were excluded from the per-protocol set, resulting in a final per-protocol set of 221 patients (92.5%) (Figure 1).

Primary End Point: MADRS at Week 8

After 8 weeks of treatment, both dosages of ammoxetine demonstrated statistically significant superiority over placebo in terms of the adjusted mean changes from baseline in the MADRS total score. The LS mean change (SE) from baseline was −16.7 (1.3) for ammoxetine 40 mg/d, −16.6 (1.3) for ammoxetine 60 mg/d, and −13. 5 (1.3) for placebo. Compared with placebo, the LS mean difference (SE) was −3.1 (1.3) for the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group (97.3% CI, −6.2 to 0.0; P = .02) and −3.3 (1.4) for the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group (97.3% CI, −6.3 to −0.3; P = .02) in the full-analysis set (Table 2). The MADRS score change from baseline over time is shown in Figure 2. In the per-protocol set, ammoxetine also achieved significant results, with an LS mean difference (SE) of −3.2 (1.4) for the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group (97.3% CI, −6.2 to −0.2; P = .02) and −3.2 (1.4) for the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group (97.3% CI, −6.2 to −0.2; P = .02) (Table 2).

Sensitivity Analysis

Based on the per-protocol set plus controlled dropout patients, the first sensitivity analysis was conducted using analysis of covariance. At the end of week 8, the LS mean difference (SE) between the ammoxetine 60 mg/d and placebo groups was −2.7 (1.4) (97.3% CI, −5.8 to 0.4; P = .025). The LS mean difference (SE) between the ammoxetine 40 mg/d and placebo groups was −3.0 (1.4) (97.3% CI, −6.1 to 0.1; P = .02) (eTable 2 in Supplement 2).

The second sensitivity analysis was conducted using a mixed-effects model with repeated measures on both the full-analysis and per-protocol sets. In the full-analysis set at week 8, the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group vs placebo demonstrated an LS mean difference (SE) of −3.5 (1.4) (97.3% CI, −6.6 to −0.4; P = .01), while that of the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group was −3.4 (1.4) (97.3% CI, −6.5 to −0.3; P = .01) (eFigure 1A in Supplement 2). Corresponding results in the per-protocol set were −3.5 (1.4) (97.3% CI, −6.6 to −0.5; P = .01) for ammoxetine 60 mg/d and −3.4 (1.4) (97.3% CI, −6.4 to −0.4; P = .01) for ammoxetine 40 mg/d (eFigure 1B in Supplement 2). Both active doses showed statistically significant superiority over placebo in both analysis sets at week 8, with results consistent with the primary analysis, thereby confirming the robustness of the treatment effect.

Subgroup Analysis

Prespecified subgroup analyses showed that among participants with baseline MADRS scores greater than 30, the LS mean (SE) from baseline to week 8 of treatment was −17.2 (1.6) points for the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, −19.5 (1.7) points for the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group, and −13.8 (1.7) points for the placebo group. Among these differences, the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group was significantly superior to the placebo group (2-sided P = .002) (eFigure 2 in Supplement 2). Additionally, in participants with recurrent MDD, the LS mean (SE) in MADRS scores from baseline to week 8 of treatment was −16.3 (1.0) points for the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, −16.1 (1.0) points for the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group, and −12.9 (1.0) points for the placebo group. Both the ammoxetine 60 mg/d and 40 mg/d groups were significantly superior to the placebo group in the full-analysis set (2-sided P = .03) (eFigure 2 in Supplement 2). The subgroup analysis results in the per-protocol set were consistent with those in the full-analysis set (eFigure 3 in Supplement 2).

Secondary End Point

Using a covariance model with baseline HAMA scores as a covariate and group and random stratification factors as fixed effects, the change from baseline to week 8 in HAMA scores for the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group was significantly greater than that of the placebo group, with an LS mean difference (SE) of −1.8 (0.9) (95% CI, −3.4 to −0.1; P = .04) (eFigure 4 in Supplement 2) indicating effective alleviation of anxiety symptoms. At the end of week 8, no significant differences were observed between the placebo group and the ammoxetine 60 mg/d and 40 mg/d groups using the HAMD-17, CGI-S, and CGI-I assessments; response and remission rates of MADRS; and the proportion of patients with an MADRS score reduction of 25% or more at weeks 1 or 2 (eTable 3 in Supplement 2).

Safety

Treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs) were reported by 68 participants (85.0%) in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, 63 (78.8%) in the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group, and 48 (60.8%) in the placebo group. Most TEAEs were mild or moderate. The most common TEAEs reported for ammoxetine included dry mouth, nausea, and dizziness (Table 3). The TEAEs in the experimental groups with an incidence twice as high as the placebo group were constipation, parageusia, weight loss, hyperhidrosis, decreased appetite, dry mouth, diarrhea, dizziness, and nausea. Alanine aminotransferase and aspartate aminotransferase increases in only 2 participants and 1 case of liver function abnormality occurred in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, and all were mild or moderate. Severe TEAEs occurred in 2 participants in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group (1 acne, 1 constipation) and 1 in the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group (constipation) vs 0 in the placebo group.

Among the treatment-emergent adverse events with an incidence of greater than 5% across various investigations, weight loss was the only one that exceeded the threshold.

In the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, 1 drug-related serious adverse event that was moderate in severity (depressive symptoms and suicidal behavior) occurred. The participant recovered but discontinued treatment and withdrew from the trial. No serious adverse events in the ammoxetine 40 mg/d or placebo groups occurred, and there were no deaths.

The number of participants with suicidal thoughts at baseline were 12 (15.0%), 6 (7.5%), and 11 (13.9%) in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d, ammoxetine 40 mg/d, and placebo groups, respectively. At 8 weeks of treatment, the numbers were 4 (5.5%), 3 (4.1%), and 2 (2.7%), respectively. At baseline, there was 1 participant each in the ammoxetine 40 mg/d and placebo groups who exhibited suicidal behavior; at 4 weeks of treatment, 1 participant each in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d and 40 mg/d groups exhibited suicidal behavior. These participants did not exhibit suicidal behavior at the remaining visits. The TEAEs that led to withdrawal from the trial included 3 participants (3.8%) in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group (euphoria, suicidal behavior, vomiting), 2 (2.5%) in the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group (suicidal thoughts, nausea), and 1 (1.3%) in the placebo group (suicidal thoughts).

After the 8-week treatment, LS mean differences (SEs) from baseline in male participants’ Arizona Sexual Experience Scale total scores were 0.1 (3.8) points in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, −0.5 (3.7) points in the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group, and −0.7 (2.5) points in the placebo group. In female participants, the differences were −1.8 (3.5), −1.8 (3.9), and −0.6 (3.0), respectively. Ammoxetine enhanced female participants’ sexual experience to a greater extent. No clinically significant changes in vital signs, laboratory tests, or electrocardiography results were observed. Both ammoxetine doses demonstrated weight reduction (Table 3).

Discussion

This phase 2 randomized clinical trial evaluated the efficacy and safety of ammoxetine in patients with MDD. After 8 weeks of treatment, both the 40 mg/d and 60 mg/d doses of ammoxetine significantly improved depressive symptoms compared with placebo, as indicated by analyses from both the full-analysis and per-protocol sets. In both analytic sets, the mean treatment differences for the ammoxetine 40 mg/d and 60 mg/d groups exceeded 3, which is considered clinically significant.29,30 These consistent results enhanced our confidence in the trial outcomes.

Additionally, we conducted 2 sensitivity analyses to verify the robustness and consistency of the primary efficacy results. The first sensitivity analysis was based on the per-protocol set while accounting for controlled dropout patients. At week 8, both doses of ammoxetine demonstrated improvements in depressive symptoms compared with placebo. Although neither group showed statistical significance, which may have been due to the conservativeness of this analysis, these results are still notable. The second sensitivity analysis confirmed significant differences at week 8 between both the ammoxetine 40 mg/d and 60 mg/d groups and the placebo group. Thus, conclusions drawn from both datasets were consistent, enhancing our confidence in the efficacy of ammoxetine.

In the subgroup analysis based on the full-analysis set, participants with baseline MADRS scores greater than 30 exhibited superior efficacy of ammoxetine 40 mg/d compared with placebo at the end of the 8-week treatment period. However, no significant differences were observed for either dose of ammoxetine in participants with MADRS scores between 26 and 30. For participants with recurrent MDD, both the ammoxetine 60 mg/d and 40 mg/d groups demonstrated statistically significant superiority over placebo. In contrast, neither ammoxetine group showed significant superiority over placebo in participants with a first episode of MDD, possibly due to a stronger placebo effect in these patients. These findings suggest that ammoxetine has a pronounced effect in treating recurrent depressive disorder, consistent with the per-protocol results.

The LS mean differences in MADRS scores from baseline to 8 weeks were 16.6 for the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group, 16.7 for the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group, and 13.5 for the placebo group. The improvement seen with ammoxetine was comparable to that reported in studies of duloxetine, vortioxetine, and vilazodone. Previous trials indicated LS mean changes of 14.3 for vortioxetine 15 mg/d, 15.6 for vortioxetine 20 mg/d, 16.9 for duloxetine 60 mg/d, and 13.3 for vilazodone 40 mg/d.31,32

The rate of TEAEs was 85.0% in the ammoxetine 60 mg/d group and 78.8% in the ammoxetine 40 mg/d group. These rates align with a meta-analysis reporting TEAE rates of 73.3% to 73.6% for escitalopram and 78.2% for other SSRIs,33 alongside 77.4% for SNRIs (including venlafaxine and duloxetine).29

In terms of discontinuation rates due to treatment-related adverse events, ammoxetine exhibited lower rates compared with other antidepressant drugs. Specifically, ammoxetine had discontinuation rates of 3.8% in in 60 mg/d group and 2.5% in the 40 mg/d group, which are lower than the average rate of 7% found in a comprehensive meta-analysis of antidepressant trials.34 Conversely, duloxetine and venlafaxine had higher discontinuation rates of approximately 9% and 12%, respectively.35,36 Compared with recently developed antidepressant drugs, vortioxetine showed rates ranging from 5% to 8% across different doses.37 As for ansofaxine, the discontinuation rates due to treatment-related adverse events were 3.8% and 7.1% at doses of 80 mg/d and 160 mg/d, respectively.29

In this study, the incidence of liver function abnormalities in the ammoxetine groups was 0.6%, with no patients discontinuing treatment due to these issues. Previous studies on duloxetine showed that alanine aminotransferase elevations greater than 3 times the upper limit of normal occurred in 1.4% of patients, and 0.3% discontinued treatment due to liver transaminase elevations.34 These findings suggest that ammoxetine has a lower risk of liver safety issues compared with duloxetine. Overall, the data indicate that ammoxetine demonstrates a comparable or potentially superior safety and tolerability profile relative to commonly used antidepressants.

The most common TEAEs reported for ammoxetine were dry mouth, nausea, and dizziness, similar to patterns observed with SSRIs and SNRIs.33,38 Sexual dysfunction, a frequent adverse effect of serotonin reuptake inhibitors, is a common reason for discontinuation. Notably, no patients discontinued treatment due to sexual dysfunction, suggesting that ammoxetine is safe and well tolerated.

Limitations

This study had some limitations. An important limitation was the small sample size, which highlights the need for further research with a larger cohort. Additionally, the exclusion of patients with comorbid conditions may have limited the generalizability of the results to typical clinical practice. Moreover, as a phase 2 proof-of-concept study, it was not designed or powered for formal dose comparisons between ammoxetine 40 mg/d and 60 mg/d; therefore, a dedicated phase 3 trial has been designed to determine the optimal dose that balances efficacy and safety.39 Finally, the absence of an active comparator group limited direct comparisons with existing antidepressant treatments.

Conclusions

This randomized clinical trial demonstrates the efficacy, safety, and tolerability of ammoxetine in patients with MDD. These results support the need for further phase 3 trial investigations, which should involve larger samples.
