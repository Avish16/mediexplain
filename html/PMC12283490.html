Redefining Failure: Criteria for Unsuccessful Outcomes in Meniscus Repair

Abstract

Purpose of Review

Meniscus repair plays a critical role in preserving knee function and delaying degenerative changes after a meniscus tear. Despite advancements in surgical techniques, there remains significant variability in how outcomes are defined and reported. This review examines the evolving interplay between traditional metrics of success such as reoperation rates, radiographic healing, etc., and the more subjective patient reported outcome measures (PROMs).

Recent Findings

Recent findings highlight the discrepancies between radiographic healing, symptomatic relief, and functional improvement. While reoperation rates remain a widely used failure criterion, they do not account for patients who avoid revision surgery despite persistent symptoms. MRI assessments can detect incomplete healing, but imaging abnormalities do not always correlate with clinical dysfunction. PROMs and return-to-sport (RTS) rates offer valuable insight into functional recovery, yet they vary across studies and may not always reflect anatomical failure. Emerging consensus supports a dual model: anatomical failure, which reflects structural compromise seen on imaging or second-look arthroscopy, and clinical failure, which includes persistent symptoms, limited function, or poor patient-reported outcomes regardless of imaging results.

Summary

Meniscus repair failure should be assessed using a multidimensional approach, incorporating structural integrity, symptom persistence, functional performance, and patient satisfaction. Standardizing failure definitions will improve data comparability, enhance treatment strategies, and guide patient expectations. Future research should refine composite failure models and integrate meniscus-specific PROMs to optimize long-term outcomes. By redefining failure, clinicians can improve surgical success rates and provide more personalized, evidence-based care.

Takeaway

Not all healed menisci function well, and not all unhealed ones fail. By redefining failure, we can reframe success—and better serve patients.

Keywords: Meniscus repair, Meniscus repair failure, Meniscus repair outcomes, Criteria

Introduction

Meniscal injuries are among the most common intra-articular knee pathologies, with meniscus repair surgery prioritized whenever possible to preserve joint stability and delay degenerative changes. However, despite the improved success and increased acceptance of meniscus repair in general, defining success and failure remains a significant challenge. Reported failure rates range widely from 0 to 75%, reflecting variability in surgical techniques, patient factors, and outcomes measured [1]. Factors such as age, sex, body mass index (BMI), knee stability, alignment, comorbidities, tear characteristics, surgical approach, implants, pre-injury activity level, and patient expectations contribute to this inconsistency [1–16]. Additionally, the diverse outcome measures used in studies make comparisons difficult [9, 11, 14, 15, 17–19]. Traditionally, meniscus repair success has been assessed through objective metrics such as reoperation rates and radiographic findings [20–22, 23]. While reoperation rates provide a straightforward measure, they fail to capture the broader patient experience. Some patients with improved, yet still with some degree of symptoms, decline further surgery despite persistent mechanical issues, while others undergo reoperation for minor or asymptomatic radiographic findings [9, 23, 24]Similarly, radiographic and MRI-based evaluations offer insights into healing but may not correlate with symptom relief or functional improvement.

Functional outcomes, including return to pre-injury activity levels, add another dimension to outcome assessment. However, these metrics are inconsistently reported and influenced by rehabilitation protocols and individual patient expectations. Subjective measures, particularly patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs), provide valuable insights into pain relief and functional recovery. Common tools such as the Knee Injury Osteoarthritis Outcome Score (KOOS), Tegner activity score, Lysholm score, Hospital for Special Surgery (HSS) score, Japanese Orthopedic Association Score, Marx activity knee score, Western Ontario Meniscal Evaluation Tool (WOMET), International Knee Documentation Committee (IKDC) subjective scores are frequently used, but their variability across studies complicates standardization and comparison. Furthermore, PROMs may not fully capture residual deficits in knee function or mechanical symptoms if the PRO is too broad and not disease specific.

This review explores the diverse outcome measures reported in the literature, critically examining their relevance and limitations. By synthesizing these findings, we aim to incorporate objective, subjective, and functional criteria, addressing the current inconsistencies in outcome reporting to enhance both clinical decision-making and research quality.

Discussion

One of the most significant challenges in assessing meniscus repair failure is the lack of standardization. Different studies define success and failure using varying criteria, making it difficult to compare outcomes across research. Some studies focus on structural healing observed through imaging [22–27], while others emphasize symptomatic relief or functional outcomes [2, 9, 13, 28–30]. In an early study from 1998, Barrett et al. used joint line tenderness, effusion, and McMurray’s test as predictors of clinical failure in 37 patients over 40 years of age. Patients were followed for a minimum of 24 months, and those presenting with one or more of these clinical signs at the latest follow-up were considered to have a clinical failure. The reported failure rate was 5 out of 37 patients (13.5%). All five patients were arthroscopically confirmed to have a persistent meniscal tear; however, three of five were found to have an acute meniscus tear unrelated to the repair site [29]. Similarly, Noyes et al. defined meniscus repair failure as the persistence of symptoms, such as swelling, locking, or joint pain, or the need for repeat knee arthroscopy and meniscectomy [19]. However, some studies have found that repeat arthroscopy can reveal partially healed menisci in patients who are asymptomatic, raising questions about the accuracy of symptom-based assessments. This inconsistency in definitions complicates data synthesis and limits the ability to develop universal treatment guidelines. Another challenge in assessing meniscus healing is the timeframe for evaluation. Healing is a dynamic process, and failure rates can change over time. Some studies focus on short-term outcomes within one to two years post-surgery, while others assess long-term results beyond five years. A recent meta-analysis by Schweizer et al., which included 4,000 patients, reported an overall meniscal failure rate of 14%, with 86% of failures occurring within the first two years after surgery [3]. Similarly, a comprehensive meta-analysis by Ow et al. examined failure rates at different follow-up intervals, reporting rates of 12% at 0–1 years, 15% at 2–3 years, and 19% at 4–6 years [15]. Notably, failure rates were higher among younger patients who returned to preinjury activity levels [15]. Studies with longer follow-up periods (minimum of five years) reported even higher failure rates beyond the second postoperative year, with rates reaching 30% and 36% [4, 6]. Additionally, a meta-analysis by Costa et al. presented that only five out of the nine included studies had follow-ups exceeding two years. It remains unclear whether reported failures resulted from a failed repair construct or a new tear; these results highlight the need for extended surveillance in future research [1].

Patient variability adds another layer of complexity to failure assessment. Individual differences in age, activity level, comorbidities, and rehabilitation adherence significantly influence clinical outcomes. Older patients and those with pre-existing cartilage damage tend to have higher failure rates, irrespective of surgical success [6, 9, 15]. However, a lower-demand patient may have a “successful” outcome even if a meniscus repair doesn’t fully heal because they place fewer demands on the knee after surgery.

There is no universally accepted definition of meniscus repair failure in the current literature. Defining failure in meniscus repair remains a challenge due to the complex interplay between structural integrity and functional outcomes. While anatomic failure refers to structural disruptions in the repaired meniscus, functional failure encompasses persistent symptoms and impaired knee function without strict regard for structural healing. Differentiating between these two types of failure is essential for accurately assessing surgical outcomes and guiding clinical decision-making (Table 2).

Reoperation Rates as a Failure Criterion

Reoperation rates are frequently used to assess meniscus repair failure [1, 3, 10, 31–34]. They indicate the need for further surgical intervention, such as repeat repair, partial meniscectomy, or total knee arthroplasty (TKA). This metric provides a clear indicator of treatment failure. This metric is widely used due to its ease of measurement and its ability to confirm failure arthroscopically. The primary advantage of using reoperation as a failure metric is its objectivity and consistency. However, the lack of differentiation between partial failures (where the meniscus still provides functional benefit) and total failures (requiring complete revision) remains a challenge [21].

Despite its objectivity, reoperation rates have limitations. Some patients with persistent symptoms may avoid further surgery, leading to an underestimation of failure rates. Conversely, some reoperations may be performed for unrelated reasons—such as new injuries or degenerative changes—potentially overestimating failure rates.

Several studies have examined factors influencing reoperation rates after meniscus repair. Steadman et al. reported higher reoperation rates in patients younger than 40 compared to older individuals. However, when excluding surgeries performed for other knee issues, such as ACL injuries or new meniscal tears, failure rates were similar across age groups [5, 35].

Even though repair failure rates may look similar, functional outcomes might differ. Sedgwick et al. conducted a systematic review and found that meniscal repair in carefully selected patients over 40 years old resulted in good success rates and patient-reported outcomes comparable to those of younger patients (< 40 years) [30]. This further suggests that reoperation rates and functional outcomes might not correlate. Age-related factors may influence meniscus healing and repair outcomes can still be favorable in older populations when appropriate patient selection is considered.

Similarly, Schweizer et al. found that 19.1% of meniscus repairs required revision surgery, with 36% of failures occurring beyond the second postoperative year [3]. These findings highlight the importance of long-term follow-up, as more failures are expected years after the initial repair. Farinelli et al. reported a 26% failure rate for medial meniscus repairs, with bucket-handle tears exhibiting the highest failure rate (69%), emphasizing that tear type and location significantly influence reoperation risk [10].

MRI and Imaging-Based Failure Criteria

MRI is a valuable tool for assessing meniscus healing, allowing for early detection of incomplete healing, retears, or meniscal extrusion. Henning et al. introduced MRI-based criteria for evaluating meniscus repair, defining healing status based on the percentage of the cleft remaining− [36. Unlike symptom-based or reoperation assessments, MRI can identify asymptomatic failures, enabling early intervention before symptoms become severe [26] if clinically indicated. However, while MRI-detected partial healing may not always correlate with clinical scores, a complete disruption of the repaired meniscus on MRI has been associated with significantly lower Pedi-IKDC scores in young patients [25].

Despite its advantages, MRI has notable limitations in postoperative evaluations. Scar tissue at the repair site frequently produces high-intensity signals, which can be mistaken for an unhealed meniscus, leading to false-positive diagnoses [37]. Additionally, MRI findings do not always align with clinical symptoms. Some patients with MRI-confirmed retears remain asymptomatic, while others report pain despite normal imaging. A systematic review by Morris et al. found an 11.7% failure rate following horizontal cleavage tear repairs diagnosed via MRI, yet some MRI-detected failures were asymptomatic or did not require revision surgery [7]. Similarly, Zhuo et al. reported that in side-to-side lateral meniscus root repairs near the root when done in conjunction with ACL reconstruction, MRI indicated 97% complete healing at 13 months postoperatively in asymptomatic patients, but second-look arthroscopy revealed a 13.6% partial healing rate in those undergoing ACL implant removal [38].

Despite these challenges, MRI remains the primary imaging modality for postoperative meniscus repair assessment, particularly in symptomatic patients. Advanced MRI techniques, such as MRI mapping, have been proposed to enhance healing evaluation, but their routine clinical use remains limited [24]. Schwach et al. suggested refining MRI interpretation by assessing signal intensity, morphological changes, and tear diastasis rather than relying solely on traditional classification systems [20]. Their findings indicate that smaller postoperative tear gaps correlate with better functional outcomes, reinforcing the relationship between MRI-based healing assessments and clinical recovery [20, 22].

Routine MRI follow-up may not always be clinically necessary, as patients with MRI-confirmed partial-healing often achieve similar functional outcomes to those with fully healed menisci [23, 25, 37]. Fauno et al. suggested that MRI accuracy in diagnosing unhealed meniscal repairs improves when fewer than three clinical symptoms persist [27]. Given these considerations, future research should focus on standardizing MRI-based healing criteria and exploring alternative imaging techniques that better correlate with functional recovery.

Clinical Symptoms as a Failure Criterion

Clinical symptoms—such as persistent knee pain, swelling, locking, and mechanical dysfunction—offer a patient-centered approach to assessing meniscus repair failure. Unlike objective measures like reoperation rates or imaging findings, clinical symptoms directly reflect functional impairment, influencing whether a patient seeks further treatment or perceives their surgery as unsuccessful. A key advantage of symptom-based failure criteria is that they capture patients who experience poor functional outcomes but do not undergo revision surgery, providing a broader perspective on treatment success.

Several studies have examined failure rates based on persistent symptoms. Dai et al. found that clinical failure was often associated with residual meniscal clefts detected on second-look arthroscopy, suggesting that unresolved structural defects contribute to ongoing symptoms [39]. Husen et al. reported that 22% of meniscus repairs in patients over 60 years failed due to persistent symptoms or reoperation, indicating that age-related degenerative changes may play a role in symptom persistence even when the meniscus remains structurally intact [9].

Studies have explored standardized clinical assessments for defining failure. Yeh et al. applied Barrett’s criteria—which assess joint tenderness, effusion, and mechanical symptoms—and reported a 14.8% failure rate [40]. Zhu et al. found that arthroscopic repair of isolated medial degenerative meniscus tears in patients over 45 years, particularly those with mechanical symptoms or failed conservative treatment, led to significant functional improvement with low clinical failure rates. However, MRI revealed unhealed menisci in 41% of patients at a mean follow-up of 4.3 years, highlighting a disconnect between imaging findings and patient-reported outcomes [2].

Similarly, the Barber-Westin and Noyes review emphasized that the absence of reoperation and symptoms indicates clinical healing. They found that persistent tibiofemoral pain and knee locking were linked to failure, but symptoms did not always correlate with imaging or arthroscopy findings. Some patients reported pain despite no structural failure, while others with MRI-confirmed abnormalities remained asymptomatic, complicating failure classification. These findings underscore the need for standardized failure criteria that integrate patient-reported symptoms, functional assessments, and objective measures like MRI and clinical tests [28].

Despite its advantages, symptom-based assessment has limitations. Symptoms are highly subjective, varying based on pain tolerance, psychological factors, and activity levels. Additionally, not all symptomatic cases indicate true meniscal failure, as biomechanical changes, joint inflammation, or residual instability can contribute to persistent symptoms without a re-tear.

Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs) and Functional Outcomes

PROMs are widely used to evaluate knee function, patient satisfaction, and symptom relief following meniscus repair. These tools provide more rigorous insight into the patient’s subjective experience, capturing perspectives on pain, mobility, and activity level restoration. Commonly used PROMs include the KOOS, the Lysholm score, IKDC subjective score, and the Visual Analog Scale (VAS) for pain. By enabling large-scale comparisons of functional outcomes across studies, PROMs help assess the effectiveness of meniscus repair from the patient’s point of view.

However, research on the correlation between PROMs and meniscus repair success has shown mixed results. Some studies report lower PROM scores in patients with failed meniscus repairs, indicating that symptom persistence negatively impacts self-reported function [2, 8, 9, 17, 26]. In contrast, other studies have found that some patients with anatomical failure still report high functional scores, suggesting that subjective outcomes may not always align with structural healing [3, 11, 40]. This variability may stem from the subjective nature of PROMs, which are influenced by patient expectations, lifestyle, and psychological factors. Some patients remain satisfied despite mechanical dysfunction, while others report dissatisfaction even when imaging confirms successful repair. Another limitation is the frequent use of general knee function PROMs that are not specific to meniscus pathology, potentially reducing their sensitivity in detecting meniscus-related symptoms.

To improve the accuracy of symptom assessment, incorporating a meniscus-specific PROM such as the WOMET score may be beneficial [41, 42]. Unlike broader knee function scores, this tool focuses specifically on meniscus-related symptoms making it more effective in evaluating localized joint instability, locking, or catching. Given that meniscal injuries often involve mechanical dysfunction rather than generalized knee impairment, these targeted PROMs could provide a more sensitive measure of residual deficits following meniscus repair. Standardizing the use of meniscus-specific PROMs could enhance failure assessment by identifying subtle yet clinically significant impairments that broader tools like KOOS or IKDC might overlook. By refining outcome measurement, these specialized tools could lead to a more accurate evaluation of repair success, helping guide treatment decisions and improving patient-centered care.

Return to sport (RTS) is a crucial outcome measure following meniscus repair, particularly in athletes seeking to regain pre-injury performance levels. Several studies have reported high short-term RTS rates after meniscus repair, even while the healing process is ongoing. Willinger et al. found that 100% of young athletes returned to sport within six months postoperatively, with 44.8% achieving their pre-injury level. However, MRI findings revealed that only 55.9% of menisci were fully healed at final follow-up, with 35.3% showing partial healing and 8.8% classified as non-healed [43]​. This highlights the discrepancy between clinical recovery and biological healing, suggesting that RTS decisions should consider both functional performance and imaging findings.

Combined Approaches

Given the limitations of isolated failure criteria, a combined approach may be superior (Fig. 1).Schweizer et al. proposed a composite failure definition integrating revision surgery, symptoms, MRI findings, and functional scores. Everhart et al. emphasized that structural failure does not always correlate with functional impairment, reinforcing the need for a multidimensional framework.

A standardized failure classification can improve treatment planning and research comparability. Future studies should refine composite failure definitions, integrate standardized functional benchmarks, and establish consensus guidelines for MRI interpretation Fillingham et al. categorized meniscal repair failures into clinical and anatomical failures. Clinical failures are characterized by persistent mechanical symptoms, such as catching, locking, or instability, along with joint effusion and joint line tenderness, indicating unresolved meniscal pathology [14]. Anatomical failures, on the other hand, refer to incomplete or absent healing of the meniscus. Anatomical failure, defined as a partial or complete meniscal tear or extrusion, is typically diagnosed via second-look arthroscopy or MRI. However, a structural failure does not always translate to functional impairment, as some patients with a partially healed or re-torn meniscus remain asymptomatic and opt out of further surgery. Clinical failure is characterized by clinical symptoms, PROMs, functional test results, and return-to-sport (RTS) rates. While clinical failure is often linked to anatomical failure, it can also arise due to subjective assessment variability, coexisting injuries, differences in rehabilitation, or patient expectations. These factors can sometimes lead to false-positive diagnoses. By integrating both structural and functional assessments, a combined failure model can provide a clearer and more patient-centered understanding of meniscus repair outcomes.

Conclusion

Meniscus repair failure is a multifaceted issue requiring a comprehensive assessment framework. Traditional success criteria—reoperation rates and imaging findings—fail to capture functional and subjective outcomes. By categorizing failure into anatomical and clinical domains, this framework provides a holistic approach to defining meniscus repair failure. Standardizing assessment criteria will enhance clinical decision-making, improve treatment strategies, and optimize long-term outcomes for patients undergoing meniscus repair. Future advancements in imaging, biomechanics, and rehabilitation protocols will further refine failure definitions, leading to improved diagnostic accuracy and patient-centered care.
